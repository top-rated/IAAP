Symbolic function

From Mario Jacoby's book "Jungian Psychotherapy and Contemporary Infant Research" (Jungian Psychotherapy and Contemporary Infant Research, Mario Jacoby)

Translation: Valentina Samus,

Editor: Dmytro Zaleskyi

An introductory word

By "symbolic function" most infant researchers mean the baby's ability to imagine other people or objects in their absence. Thanks to this ability, thinking is detached from concrete perception and opens the way to free fantasy that does not depend on literal reality, and can even deny it. The baby now has the opportunity to imagine and wish for a different reality than the one that is. But until this maturation phase is reached, perception is shifted in the direction of current circumstances, such as affective and cognitive needs, without the possibility of imagining that what is perceived can be different from what it is (see Dornes, 1993, p. 193). Transforming ideas about concrete reality into the world of fantasy or imagination is not yet possible for an infant. This exclusively human ability to fantasize, on which our creativity is based and, after all, our entire culture and civilization first unfolds at about 18 months. By this age, "for the infant, his experience has obvious features of inevitability: it cannot be other than what it is" (Ogden, 1984, p. 187). Thus, wishful thinking is impossible for the "pre-symbolic" infant, since wishing is an intrapsychic construct that is itself "constituted by means of a symbolic function." Cognitive symbols

It should be noted here that by "symbolization" infant researchers mean the image of a memory of an absent object. The caring caretaker, or, as psychoanalysts unpleasantly put it, the "object", becomes a content in the inner world of the imagination, an attainable image that the infant can evoke. This is described as "symbolic representation" and is the premise of any mental image or representation of another or self. It is this ability that usually develops before the age of 18 months.

As mentioned earlier, this concept of symbol is not the same as what Jung and analytical psychology understand by symbol; in modern psychoanalysis, symbols also have a more complex meaning. What infant researchers talk about are so-called "cognitive symbols" (see Dornes, 1993, p. 184), which represent, in our view, people, things, facts that are momentarily or permanently absent from the external world. However, the presented content is quite familiar and thus corresponds to real perception. It can be called verbally, and we have its image in our head. With the help of verbal definitions, we can convey this meaning to the consciousness of other people. Thus, language largely consists of cognitive symbols.

Psychoanalytic symbols

"Psychoanalytic" symbols, on the contrary, point to what is repressed from consciousness and, therefore, unconscious. The question of whether psychoanalytic symbols in the development of an infant begin to act earlier (at 6-12 months) than cognitive symbols (at 18 months) remains open. However, infant researchers are of the opinion that cognitive or semantic symbolism should be seen as a necessary condition for a symbol to be understood psychoanalytically. (see Dornes, 1993, p. 105). In addition, the visual representation of repressed content using psychoanalytic symbols implies the existence of internal images. Otherwise, it would be impossible, for example, to symbolize the penis through the image of a snake, or the female genital organs in the form of a vase. Therefore, the existence of cognitive or semantic symbols is, with all probability, a prerequisite for the appearance of psychoanalytic symbols.

But in psychoanalytic theory there is already another version of the concept of symbolization, which serves as an alternative to the concept of repressed content. Guided by the logic of Melanie Klein's previous work on the formation of symbols (Klein, 1930) and Winnicott's theory of the transitional object (1965), comes the understanding of symbolization as the giving of psychological meaning. Symbolization is not absolutely equivalent to graphic expression or representation. Every adult has the ability to express or depict something graphically, but there are many people who cannot "symbolize". Thus, many people suffering from psychosomatic diseases are unable to experience the variety of sensations of their own body, as well as affects, internal images, and thoughts as the ambiguity of their mental life. Feelings and thoughts are for them separate fragments of a fully specified character. Although they ideally able to imagine on the inner screen something that is not physically present, their attempts to fantasize or even feel their own body are empty and stereotyped. The ability to reproduce images in them is preserved, so the experience of vividness, fluidity or meaning associated with any image or affect is rather lost. Therefore, such a person cannot integrate images or affects into a coherent inner world. Thus, this deficiency manifests itself in their inability to give personal meaning to experience, hence to get a chance for any creative path to the imagination. Jungian vision of symbols

With these last observations we come closer to the Jungian view of symbols. Jung distinguishes between the symbol proper and what he calls a sign or allegory. Signs and allegories are referential; that is, point to certain facts that may be generally known and understood. A factual symbol, on the contrary, is for Jung "an optimal designation or formula for a relatively unknown ... fact" (Jung, 1921, p. 814). In contrast to the previous psychoanalytic point of view, Jung is interested here not only in what was repressed, but mainly in the symbol as a form of expression of the unconscious's own creative power. Thus, the symbol stands, so to speak, on the threshold between conscious perceptions or ideas and the influence of processes emanating from the unconscious. As such, he participates in both spheres, combining both conscious and unconscious aspects. So,

So, it becomes obvious how symbols are an integral part of any religion. They can constellate numinous experiences, giving form or image to an otherwise indescribable, "transcendent" realm. Pointing in the direction of a transcendent dimension, they can evoke a sense of reverent awe in religiously receptive people. Indeed, from a psychological point of view, the realm of the transcendent can mean no more than a dimension that goes beyond human ego-consciousness and is equivalent to the unconscious. Depth psychology recognizes that the power of the unconscious affects our conscious realm and can express itself in symbols of radiant numinousness. As an example, the declaration of the "truth" of any religious belief lies outside the boundaries of psychology. However, beliefs and numinous experiences may be open to psychological investigation. Thus, the symbols that have a numinous character will serve as a means of communication with the transcendental dimension. ("Symbol" comes from the Greek verb "symballein", which in English means literally "to throw together.").

As mentioned above, for Jung, a symbol, while it is alive, is "an expression of something that cannot be characterized in another or better way." However, a symbol is only alive if:

...it is pregnant with content. But as soon as its meaning is born, as soon as an expression is found that formulates the thing that was sought, expected or heralded better than the hitherto accepted symbol, then the symbol dies, that is, it acquires only a historical meaning. (Jung, 1921, par. 816)

For this reason, it is absolutely impossible to create a living symbol, that is, one that is pregnant with meaning, only from already known associations. For what is thus formed never contains more than has been put into it‚Äù (Jung, 1921, para. 817). However, Jung also acknowledges, and this seems to me to be crucial:

Whether a thing is a symbol or not depends mainly on the attitude of the observer's consciousness; for example, regarding whether this fact is considered not simply "as such", but also as an expression of something else, something unknown. (Jung, 1921, par. 818)

Jung expresses all this relatively prosaically, so it appears from his formulation that the above attitude of the observing consciousness and its openness to the symbolic dimension may be the result of a conscious choice. This is understandable, since for Jung, who was homo religiosus by nature, such an attitude was obvious. He was fully convinced of the deep meaning of symbolic language, but often generalized or assumed too much in relation to others. Because of this, many classical Jungians also, time after time, try to convince their analysands of the content and significance of the unconscious and its symbolic language, only dooming themselves to the most counterproductive, didactic style of work. The ability to symbolize creatively cannot be taken for granted because it may be underdeveloped due to conditions of early damage.

Here we are faced with the question: "How does this specifically human capacity for symbolic imagination arise?" In any case, it is an ability inextricably linked to creativity. Although we do not know exactly what is the source of creative fantasy in an individual, it is certainly inconceivable without the ability to symbolize. The fantasy process, of course, requires full cooperation between the so-called cognitive-semantic symbolization and the capacity for symbolic imagination, the latter of which is most clearly postulated in depth psychology. In other words, reality must first of all have an internal representation. It must be presented in such a way that a mental image of it can be evoked at any moment, even when it cannot be perceived directly at the present moment. At the same time, such symbolic and cognitive representations can constantly acquire new meanings. They are pregnant with meaning, as Jung said. For example, the image of the mother is often endowed with great power, so her presence can indicate all-encompassing containment, safety, and protection. Or it can take the form of an intrusive, repulsive or devouring figure. Over time, this imaginary representation of the mother accumulates its own sense of history, shaped by the actual rich interaction of the mother with the child. Thus, "mother" becomes a symbolic image with all the ambiguity of emotional implications and meanings.

Even if the child has not yet developed the ability for symbolic fantasy, there are still his early emotional perceptions, which have merged into various RUVs, which mostly serve as an affective background for further fantasies. It is not easy for Jungians to accept the statement that a person exists in a "pre-symbolic" phase, at least during the first twelve to eighteen months of life. Only with the development of language, which occurs in the second half of the second year, does the ability to symbolic representation appear. According to Lichtenberg, this ability largely serves to build organizational structures designed to cope with the turbulent, conflict-ridden, "demonic" aspects of one's life experience (Lichtenberg, 1983, p. 130). The fact that these aspects of the child's experience (in fact, the experience of the whole life) are later symbolically reflected in dreams, dreams, free associations and spontaneous play, he rightly considers a unique discovery of psychoanalysis. Lichtenberg also emphasizes that traumatic events can be organized in these symbolically represented forms beginning at the eighteenth month (ibid.). Here, Lichtenberg obviously combines a cognitive-semantic concept (language) and a psychoanalytic one.

Lyrical Digression: Cognitive Symbols and the Jungian View of Symbols

I would like to try to demonstrate how language, which consists of cognitive symbols, is related to symbols in the Jungian sense. Language, as our way of communication, provides a coherent designation of individual objects or facts. There is an assumption that for both partners in communication, this designation evokes a corresponding image; otherwise, the word remains an empty sequence of sounds. The signified and the signified must more or less coincide, both in the mind of the speaker and the listener. With such correspondence, language can become a means of communication and mutual understanding. Since language deals with the signification of concrete objects, where there is a coincidence or correspondence between the signifier and the signified, a communicative exchange takes place. However, the difficulty comes with emotional tones, which are often difficult to put into words and which the other person may not understand. So, language begins to take on a more symbolic or metaphorical character. Someone can say, for example: "I'm in despair." The word "despair" denotes a feeling that can only be understood if you know from experience how exactly it is experienced. And even then, one cannot be sure whether the same nuances are invested by both, or the same level of intensity of the feeling of despair.

In any case, this sentence - "I am in despair" - indicates the corresponding affective state. Perhaps a person is in despair, guesses about the reasons for his condition. Or, instead, despair covers her completely, and leaves no chance of understanding what is happening. Despair often coincides with a sense of impasse. It seems that there is no way out. In such a state, an image of the path may appear, so a person is left with the question of whether to continue walking along it, or to leave this matter behind. Or the path continues endlessly, but there is no strength to go. Should I turn back, hoping to find another way? What should I do? I can't find a way out, I'm desperate. Such images may be associated with feelings of despair, and it is also possible that they will appear similarly in dreams, symbolizing this affective state.

A whole cluster of images can be evoked by the line "I am in despair." For example, I can see myself as a baby who wants to joyfully interact and contact with the mother. But the mother looks away. Perhaps she is sinking into her own despair? She hardly notices my need, she is not present. Today, we are beginning to understand, especially through infant studies, that this may be an example of a chronic deficit in emotional exchange between mother and infant. Thus, the child may feel abandoned and rejected. Babies, when they repeatedly fail to attract their mother's attention, sooner or later become discouraged. They cannot grasp what caused the lack of love: either the mother's limitations (such as her self-doubt or despair) or their own limitations.

Perhaps behind this state of despair there is something else, which remains in most cases deeply unconscious, but is expressed in the well-known collective myth of the Western world. Despair essentially means a feeling of falling out of harmony with God, the world, and oneself. In other words, we are dealing with the myth of paradise lost. In this myth, doubt is caused by the fact that the perfection of the world order created by God is called into question. And of course, it is the serpent with a forked tongue who appears as God's adversary. The snake symbolizes, among other things, the possibility, embedded in human nature, to sow the shadow of such doubt. It poisons the very pleasure of simply existing in harmony with the world and oneself. As such, it represents the deeply rooted human instinct to question again and again the validity of various prohibitions, taboos, postulates of faith and value systems. From the standpoint of the old system of values, the "conservative" internal order, it is a destructive evil. On the other hand, if viewed from within the flow of life, such doubt can also be healing and necessary, and cause a reorientation of a person's entire life.

The feeling of "I'm in despair" ultimately means that I've lost the opportunity to deal fruitfully with my doubts. Not that I have doubts, but that I am completely immersed in doubts. These doubts overwhelm me, they suppress my sense of self-worth and my drive to live. I remain disillusioned with justice and values, they are meaningless and give me no hope. The word "despair" expresses an inner state that is experienced emotionally, and can also be expressed symbolically, for example, with images. When emotions are expressed through images, it is easier to form a conscious attitude to deal with them. In this sense, dreams can be quite valuable.

Regarding the maturation of the ability to symbolize

Infant researchers, who are primarily interested in healthy or normal development and only secondarily in looking at disorders, suggest that the capacity for symbolization, which is the basis for fantasy, develops spontaneously according to a maturational schedule. Of course, caring caregivers are expected to facilitate this development as best they can, or at least not to interfere, consciously or unconsciously. Fantasies about the baby play a major role. In any case, the development of the capacity for symbolization depends largely on mirroring and the optimal dose of stimulation from the environment‚Äîa topic we explore in more detail in Chapter 11.

However, it is also worth noting Winnicott's observation that an infant, in the absence of a mother, can experience a piece of blanket, later, a doll or a teddy bear, "as if" they contain a maternal aspect. With good enough mothering, the infant knows how to distinguish between the actual presence of the mother and this "as if" function, which Winnicott called a "transitional object." The transitional function of the symbol is also illustrated by the use of the term "as if" (Gordon, 1993). The actual world and the imaginary world are connected - but not merged - thanks to the awareness "as if" the symbolic character of the image is not blurred. It makes sense to assume that productive access to the world of symbols in their "as if" sense is rooted in the infant's undisturbed ability to create and use transitional objects (Winnicott, 1965).
