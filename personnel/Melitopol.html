Melitopol is Ukraine

I am from Melitopol. ⠀

I was born and lived there until I was 18 years old. I heard the Ukrainian language in the lessons of the Ukrainian language and literature. Ukrainian traditions and everyday life did not surround me. The only feeling of Ukraine is when my mother sang "Oh in the cherry orchard" and when my father and brother watched football, rooting for Ukraine. ⠀

All. The conclusion is a feeling of marginality that lingered. ⠀

I think that's why I set out on a journey called "Who am I?". She took with her my favorite "horse" - intellectualization (one of the mechanisms of psychological protection) and began to write a dissertation on national self-awareness. ⠀

In this work, I asked myself the question, what is the miraculous way to form national self-consciousness in such a way that it is formed (strange question, right?). ⠀

It is clear that this question was to itself. Because my internal deficiency did not give me peace. Feeling of lack of identity. A gaping hole in the middle of oneself is a very unpleasant feeling. ⠀

And here I delve into dozens of theories, what kind of phenomenon is this, what are its characteristics, how does ethnic consciousness differ from national consciousness, national consciousness from self-consciousness, where is the place of national identity that unites Ukrainians, what is the role of language, what is the basis - ethnicity or nationality, etc. etc. ⠀

This is the stuff of scientific methodology. Very little is connected with the heart and living love for Ukraine. But I'll be honest, I "prepared" this Ukraine up and down. And she always came across her sore spot -  the regional mentality. I knew (not from books) that the regions of Ukraine have their own historical and socio-cultural features. And she understood that it is necessary to form national self-awareness, taking into account these features. Because according to the results of the study, we all had low national self-esteem. But the eastern and southern regions showed the highest indicators of inferiority. Their low self-esteem as Ukrainians was in no way comparable to the self-esteem of Ukrainians in the central and western regions. ⠀

But I already knew it on my own skin... ⠀

Another vividly unpleasant feeling that complemented my "non-identity" was envy. Envy of those who know what it is to be Ukrainian. And he shouts about it to the whole country. And the feeling of "non-Ukrainianness" grew stronger. ⠀

And this made it even worse.

I thought so - "Ukrainian Ukrainians" are cool. Their identity is constantly nourished by culture, language, and tradition. They seem to be permanently connected to the power grid. And we had constant interruptions in the supply of Ukrainian energy.

I know it's a bad idea to play the game of "who gets hurt more". Yes, all of Ukraine was terribly traumatized by a psychopathic aggressor neighbor, suffered from violence and humiliation for centuries. But my region was subjected to this invisible torture without interruption.

Here is the principle of simple geography. Whoever is closer to evil interacts with it more. It's like living every day next to a rapist or visiting him once a year. There is a difference.

We had our own electricity network - only we were not fed from it, but we were constantly "struck" by the current of Russian psychosis.

But this is one hundred percent childish position - to think that someone is lucky, and I am not. When you want to say - "your parents loved you, did not beat you, bought you candy and took you to the sea." From time to time I even got angry with the "beloved ones in Ukrainian". Not now. Because I have recovered, and I have a medical history that I can "wave in front of my nose". It's like such childishness (compensatory) too - to show off that I "won" this Ukrainianness, while you, they say, hothouse children simply grew up in it) Stupidity, I know...)

When I started the search for identity, I believed that there must be a way to feel my Ukrainianness. I knew for sure that the path "from the outside to the middle" did not work well for such "lost" Ukrainians like me.

Wearing a wreath and saying the affirmations before going to sleep "I am a Ukrainian" in Ukrainian did not work. It's like in psychology - buy the course "I am beautiful, happy, successful" and listen to it every day. The effect is like valerian. I understood very well that I would not become a doctor just because I put on a doctor's cap.

I didn't immediately understand what I needed to do, but I did. We were in an injury that didn't look like it. Violence that has no external manifestations. But it slowly eats away from the inside.

Trauma leads to such a state in which you "hang" between those who are relatively healthy (but you can't reach them yet)  and those who are violent (and there is a risk of "leaning in" to them, because they are strong = actually sadistic) ).

The victim identifies with the aggressor. Often. The victim "carries" his rapist in himself. This is a law of psychology.

We had a part of Ukraine that was recovering and rebuilding after the Russian trauma, and we also had a part that was "in trauma". And few people wanted to look at "this place". But I and millions of Ukrainians were there.

It is clear that long and painstaking work with collective trauma was required. Be careful, without "threats" and accusations of "bad Ukrainianness". But long-term therapy was not destined to happen, it was interrupted by an urgent "operation"... Now there is no need to think and guess, now everything can be seen, because they have already opened it. Ukraine is on the surgical table, the incision is huge, the blood is gushing, there is no anesthesia...

But if there was no operation, differentiation would be the main method of recovery. That's when you understand where I am, and where I'm not. And where it's just a piece of shit that stuck. It is important to understand that there is a healthy part, and there is an affected part. This distinction should give a chance. But sometimes this piece sticks so much that it grows. And you can tear it off only with blood...

It is not our fault when violence is committed against us. But we are responsible. To prevent this rapist from getting under the skin and becoming the full owner of the psyche.

Realizing yourself as a Ukrainian and getting rid of the imprint of trauma from "Russian psychosis" is important, it is a path "from the inside to the outside." Ideally, this process should take place in a symbolic way. For example, the currently popular "kill the Muscovite in you" is a symbolic plane. Because this is work with the internal ("where do I have Russian views", "where do I react like my psychopath-neighbor", etc.).

This is such a removal, it is painful, but it is symbolic, internal, step-by-step. In analytical psychotherapy, this is one of the main tasks - "to transfer the literal into the sphere of the symbolic." When the symbolic does not work, the energy goes into the layer of the literal. And that's why now "kill the Muscovite in yourself" is a good trend, but a little late, in my opinion) Because it's already "literally" at the helm... It's being done...

I don't really like pathetic phrases, but I really like one. Birth happens twice - the first time you are born, the second time - you are born yourself. So I was born Ukrainian. Itself. From the Ukrainian spirit.

PS ...  I wanted to write that Melitopol is Ukraine. But, as they used to say in my school years, "it's clear"
