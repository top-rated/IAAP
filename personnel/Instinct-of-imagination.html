"Instinct" of imagination. A neuroethological approach to the evolution of the reflexive mind and its application in psychotherapy

TRANSLATION:

KATERYNA ZAMKOVSKA

SCIENTIFIC AND PROFESSIONAL EDITORIAL:

IRYNA SEMKIV AND VALENTINA SAMUS

Antonio Alcaro1* and  Stefano Carta2

1Department of Psychology, Sapienza University of Rome, Rome, Italy

2 Department of Pedagogy, Psychology, and Philosophy, University of Cagliari, Cagliari, Italy

Contemporary  neuro -psychoanalytic literature emphasizes that our subjective identity is based on primitive subcortical neuropsychological processes that express non-thinking forms of experience that are "affectively intense without being known"  ( Solms and Panksepp, 2012 ). Deprived of internal representations, the emotional states of our "core-Self"  ( Panksepp  , 1998b ) are "projected" to the outside world, being discharged, as a rule, through instinctive patterns of behavior. However, because of the close connections between the subcortical and cortical centers of the midbrain, emotional drives can also find a way to be reflected through internal self-referential processing, which becomes evident when the organism is not actively interacting with the outside world. Due to this endogenous functioning, the emotional dispositions of the core-Self are not clearly realized, instead, they are organized within the bounds of connected dynamic mental structures, which KG Jung called "emotionally colored complexes" and Melanie Klein - "unconscious phantasies   "  . The internal self-referential dynamics of the "brain-psyche" ( "brainmind") originates  from the state of excitement in the REM sleep stage, then unfolds in the activation of the cortico-limbic midbrain complex (CMS) in a state of relaxed (resting) arousal, also known as the passive brain network (DMN). From our neuroethological point of view, it is supported by the "introverted" activity of SEEKING , which leads to the subjective exploration of internally constructed virtual scenarios. This "mind-wandering" function, associated with dreaming, fantasy processing, remembering, and thinking, is the essence of the imagination function and constitutes the first form of reflection, in which intentions and drives take the primary form of conscious (but not self-conscious)  representation. During postnatal development, this original ("archetypal") function of imagination is slowly adjusted in the relational "transitional" space and can be expressed first in nonverbal, and later in abstract-verbal social communicative patterns. Our perspective has notable implications for psychotherapy. Instead of trying to directly modify interpersonal external relationships (a top-down approach), dysfunctional emotional-relational patterns can be modified through a process in which the patient is helped to let go of perceived feeling-objects in favor of immersion, through actual experiences, from the surface level perception to an empty experience-a state in which there are no images. INTRODUCTION

Most researchers today consider subjectivity (personal experience) as the result of an individual historical process, and therefore as an asset of human development. In particular, the emergence of mental life and the sense of "Self" is associated with emotional and cognitive skills that allow the child to reflect on his experience ( Damasio, 2010 ). Such a meaningful function, called "mentalizing" or "reflective function," is commonly thought to be a product of attachment relationships ( Stern, 1985 ;  Schore, 1994 ;  Fonagy and Target, 1997 ;  Fonagy et al., 2002).). Cognitive psychologists, psychoanalysts and neuroscientists agree that the child learns to have a subjective mental life and considers it his own way of assimilating experiences and aspects of the attachment relationship depending on the capacity of the attachment object (caregiver) for self-reflection (Bowlby, 1969 /1982 ;  Bretherton and Munholland, 1999 ).

Recognizing the importance of such theories, we believe that strict views of development do not take into account that newborns are endowed with an innate potential for endogenous mental activity, where the intersubjective environment is experienced and represented subjectively. Indeed, neurodevelopmental studies have shown that human embryos already possess the intrinsic brain dynamism characteristic of REM sleep, which appears before non-REM sleep and activates wakefulness, which largely predominates in the last trimester of pregnancy (Birnholz , 1981). Interestingly, REM sleep leads to endogenous activation of the subcortical midbrain complex (SCMS) and cortical midbrain structures (CMS), which are involved in highly emotional self-referential processing. Moreover, recent neurometabolic studies have shown that CMS exhibit high levels of metabolic activity not only during REM sleep, but also in a state of relaxed (relaxed) alertness, when the body is not interacting with the environment (Raichle, 2015 ) .

Endogenous self-referential processing of the CMS is related to the internal function of the generator of virtual models, with the help of which the organism forms conclusions about the structure of (its) reality ( Hobson and Friston, 2012 ). During REM sleep, this process of inference can be subjectively experienced in the form of perceptually similar images, which we call dreams, and which express a primitive form of consciousness, or proto-consciousness (Hobson, 2009 ) . During a relaxed state of alertness, this process can be subjectively experienced as an internal flow of ideas and thoughts ( Carhart-Harris and Friston, 2010 ;  Schacter et al., 2012 ;  Agnati et al., 2013 ;  Fox et al., 2013), which is clearly reminiscent of what William James described as a “stream of consciousness”:

"When we take [...] a general view of the amazing flow of our consciousness, the first thing that strikes us  is  the different pace of its parts. Like the life of a bird, it seems to consist of alternating flights and landings [...] Places of landings [...] can be kept before the mind for an indefinite time [...] Places of flights [...] appear between questions that are considered in a comparatively relaxed period (James, 1890 ) .

Therefore, following such neuroscientific directions and in accordance with Jung's analytical psychology ( Jung, 1937 ), we believe that the so-called "reflective function" is implanted in the embryonic form of mental activity, based on the "autonomous" activity of the brain, which is in a state of rest (relaxed condition). Arising from this instinct of reflection, the internal emotional dispositions of a living organism are maintained in a "potential state" and instead of being expressed in actions (motor or physiological), they close in on themselves and ultimately form a subjective mental dimension ( Jung, 1937 ;  Shamdasani, 2003). Such an important self-referential function, manifested in dreams, memorization, thinking and fantasy processing, generates images that act as the first phenomenological building blocks of our nature, the nature of animals that are endowed with a psyche. Indeed, as Jung suggested, "all that we are conscious of is an image, and this image is the psyche ... [which] is the world in which the Ego is contained" ( Jung, 1967 , p.75).

Therefore, in this paper we present a neuroethological perspective ( MacLean, 1990 ;  Panksepp, 1998b ;  Panksepp and Biven, 2012 ) that integrates evidence from human and animal studies showing how the "function of imagination" evolved in terrestrial vertebrates and what type processes in the brain are related to it. In the final part of the manuscript, we discuss some implications of our perspective for clinical psychology and psychotherapy.

DMN RESTING-STATE ACTIVITY AND SELF-REFERENT PROCESSING

In 1997,  Shulman et al. (1997)  published a meta-analytic study that indicated brain regions with increased blood flow during passive viewing of an array of stimuli compared to areas with increased blood flow during active tasks. Areas of the brain that demonstrate a high metabolism at rest are closely interconnected from an anatomical and functional point of view, belong to the CMS complex, and are defined as a  network of the passive mode of the brain  ( Raichle et al., 2001 ;  Raichle and Snyder, 2007). As Rahicle showed, "the DMN of the brain consists of separate, bilateral, and symmetrical regions of the cortex, in the medial and lateral parietal, medial prefrontal, and medial and lateral temporal cortices of humans, nonhuman primates, cats, and rodents" ( Raichle , 2015 , p. 433).

It was shown that the DMN is characterized by high intrinsic activity in the resting state during visual fixation or with eyes closed, activity decreases when subjects perform goal-directed tasks ( Gusnard et al., 2001 ;  Raichle et al., 2001 ;  Greicius et al., 2003 ). As a result, the DMN has been associated with internally-oriented mental processes ( Mason et al., 2007 ) and its functioning is considered to counterbalance the dorsal attention network (DAN), which is activated when attention is directed to the external world 1  ( Corbetta et al., 2008 ). Moreover, resting DMN activity is particularly evident during REM sleep, an evolutionarily old brain state characterized by intense neurophysiological arousal that is accompanied by isolation of the organism from the outside world (Nir and Tononi, 2010 ) .

Since the vast majority of the brain's metabolism is directed at fueling the DMN at rest (relaxation), this disproportionate consumption of energy not used for active interaction with the environment has led to the dark energy analogy in cosmology. As   Zhang and Raichle write ( Zhang and Raichle, 2010 , p. 15):

"Like our cosmos, the brain also has its own 'dark energy.' Indeed, the "visible" elements of brain activity—neuronal responses to environmental demands—make up less than 5% of the brain's energy budget, leaving the majority for internal neuronal signaling" ( Zhang and Raichle, 2010 , p. 15) .

Therefore, it remains an open question among researchers to find out what psychological functions are associated with such internal functioning of the brain. In an effort to answer this question, a growing body of human research has shown that the DMN is critical for intrinsic processing, i.e., the production of stimuli and self-related activities  2  ( Northoff, 2011 ,  2013 ,  2016 ). For example, functional brain imaging studies show that the DMN is strongly engaged when presented with personal stimuli such as a person's name compared to unrelated stimuli such as another person's name (Qin and Northoff , 2011). Most surprisingly, DMN activity does not change when we compare self-related activity with resting activity ( D'Argembeau et al., 2005 ). This "rest-self overlap" ( Northoff, 2016 ) strongly suggests that activity in a state of rest is somehow related to the processing of self-related information.

Studies of the self-referential activity of the DMN have radically changed our understanding of the brain. Until the 1990s, research in cognitive psychology and neuroscience was dominated by a problem-oriented view of mental functioning, and emphasized the view of the "brain-psyche" ("brainmind") as an organ that responds to enteroceptive and/or exteroceptive stimuli. However, the massive presence of endogenous activity means a tendency to form self-organized dynamic patterns ( Northoff, 2013 ,  2014a , b ,  2015a , b ,  2016 ,  2018 ;  Northoff and Stanghellini, 2016 ; but see also  Brown, 2002 ;  Llinás, 2002). This also means that the "brain-psyche" ("brainmind") not only reacts to stimuli, but also integrates them into its endogenous "internal" functioning ( Northoff et al., 2010a ;  Northoff and Stanghellini, 2016 ;  Northoff, 2018 ). .

From the Jungian point of view, endogenous mental activity (the Self) is formed by potential mental organizers who give external objects their emotional and cognitive value/meaning. Such organizers are derived from archetypal schemas that have general validity for the interpretation of perceived stimuli, which will gradually overlap with external experience and perception to form personalized complexes (see next paragraph). After Jung, as well as Melanie Klein's unconscious fantasies ( Klein, 1921 ,  1952/2018 ;  Isaacs, 1948 ), or  Winnicott's "fictional objects" (1967) expressed the idea of ​​endogenous psychological structures that correspond to perceived objects, and which are ultimately preserved in the form of memories belonging to coherent episodes and narratives  3 .

AFFECTIVE-EMOTIONAL BASIS OF SELF-REFERENT ACTIVITY DMN

The latest neuroscientific data indicate that the degree of self-connection depends on the functioning of the nucleus of the ventral part of the DMN ( D'Argembeau et al., 2005 ), which is specifically involved in providing an emotional and affective background within the limits of internal orienting mental activity ( Christoff et al. ., 2016 ). In other words, the ventral part of the DMN "limits" the activity of the imagination within the boundaries of certain semantic orbits, focused on characteristic emotional feelings or moods.

The ventral-nuclear part of the CMS includes the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (VMPFC), the medial orbitofrontal cortex (MOFC), the sub- and pregenual part of the anterior cingulate cortex (PACC). These regions are tightly connected to subcortical areas (brainstem, midbrain, and basal forebrain), as well as to the amygdala, basal ganglia (striatum and nucleus accumbens), and all primary exteroceptive sensory modalities (Northoff et al., 2006 ;  Christoff et al., 2016 ). In particular, affective-emotional reasoning carried out by the ventral part of the DMN depends on close connections with the SCMS complex ( Northoff et al., 2006) that are involved in basic visceral-homeostatic regulation, as well as in the emergence of instinctual drives and basic emotional dispositions ( Panksepp, 1998a , b ,  2005 ,  2010 ,  2011 ;  Damasio, 1999 ;  Denton, 2006 ;  Alcaro et al., 2017 ).

Given their primary functions, SCMS have been called the "core-Self"   ( Panksepp, 1998b ), or "proto-Self" ( Damasio, 1999 ). They are involved in the emergence of the primary form of affective consciousness, characterized by moods, somato-visceral states and basic emotional sensations, which constitute the first form of self-orientation in the world ( Panksepp, 1998b ; Damasio, 1999 ; Merker, 2007 ; Solms  and Panksepp  , 2012  ; Alcaro  and Panksepp, 2014 ;  Alcaro et al., 2017). This primitive form of "anoetic" awareness has been defined as "a rudimentary state of autonomous awareness […], with a fundamental form of first-person 'self-experience' that relies on experiential affective states and raw sensory and perceptual mental formations" (Vandekerckhove and Panksepp, 2009 , p. 1).

Fixing its activity within certain affective pathways, the ventral DMN deploys an important stabilizing function for the emergence of a stable sense of self and external reality. Interestingly, clinical observations have shown that patients with lesions of the ventral DMN have profoundly disorganized psychotic-like mental activity ( Solms and Solms, 2000 ) and remain unable to develop a coherent model of self ( Damasio, 1999 ;  Schore, 2003 ,  2009 ). In our opinion, this can be seen as a consequence of the loss of communication between the DMN and the SCMS. In such a case, the function of imagination loses its core-Self affective basis, necessary for its coherence, integration, stability and self-connection.

On the other hand, when the strength of the affect is excessively high, the directionality of mental activity can be severely limited. This leads to rumination, when fixed thoughts or ideas that dominate the mental realm compulsively arise. This phenomenon is especially evident in depression, a pathological mental state characterized by hyperemotional ruminative thoughts and increased activity at rest in the cortical-subcortical structures of the midline of the brain ( Alcaro et al., 2010 ;  Northoff et al., 2011 ).

Surprisingly, K. G. Jung long ago understood the absolute importance of affective processes for self-connection, he wrote that:

"Each mental process has a valuable quality, namely its tonality of feelings. This indicates the extent to which the process affects the subject, or how much the process means to the subject (to the extent that the process reaches consciousness at all). It is thanks to "affect" that the subject becomes involved and thus begins to feel the full weight of reality"  ( Jung, 1959 : para. 61) .

According to Jung, affects carry not only an inner subjective quality, but also an essential integrative property. Indeed, he believed that mental life is organized around specific dynamic structures called "sensory-colored complexes" that unite different mental contents on the basis of a common affective state (see also Wilkinson, 2006  ) . Each complex is united by the same affect, which defines its semantic core and organizes experience, perception, fantasies and thoughts around the central theme. For example, a specific inferiority complex can be created through a constellation of memories, thoughts and fantasies related to a lack of self-worth, self-doubt and self-doubt, as well as a feeling of not meeting standards.

The Jungian theory of sensory-colored complexes is an improvement of Pierre Jeanet's work on autonomous fixed ideas. According to  Janet (1889) , fixed ideas are mental images or thoughts that have a high emotional charge and acquire exaggerated dimensions, so they cannot be properly integrated into the Ego-consciousness and become isolated from the usual personality, creating dissociative states of the psyche (Monahan , 2009). However, unlike Jeanet's original theory, Jung argued that the dissociative aspect of complexes is usually reversible, so that they can be significantly more integrated according to the current situation. Only in severe mental pathologies, such as psychosis, certain complexes are finally dissociated from the conscious Ego and the personality is fragmented. Such disconnection of connections exposes the subject to the powerful and unmodulated activity of "his" complexes, and the subject is anankastically forced to respond to his endogenous affective, archaic images, almost devoid of volitional activity and proper working memory.

THE ESSENCE OF THE IMAGINATION FUNCTION: MIND WANDERING AS AN INTROVERTED SEARCH

The high affective self-referential activity of the DMN in the resting state can act completely unconsciously, influencing the individual's attitude towards the environment and the subjective (affective) meaning given to external experiences. Such unconscious processing has been described by clinical psychologists using the concept of the "implicit unrepressed unconscious" ( Fonagy et al., 2002 ), or "internal working models" ( Bowlby, 1969/1982 ;  Stern, 1985 ). However, self-referential brain processing can also become accessible to subjective awareness in the process of active imagination, which illuminates the internal dispositions of the Self and its prediction models ( Alcaro and Panksepp, 2014). Conscious representations that arise relate to objects that were not actually perceived by the exteroceptive sense organs, but which are subjectively constructed in order to express and represent the internal dispositions of a living organism (instincts, drives, habits, emotions) and their connections connection with current prognostic operational working models (see definition of images and image schemes in Appendix 1  Appendix 1 ).

When self-referential processing joins subjective awareness, our mental sphere is filled with perceptually similar exteroceptive images with predominantly (but not exclusively) visuospatial features. The product of this reflexive instinct is an outpouring of images (and thoughts), which, like jets of pure water from the rocks of a mountain stream, are born on the border between the unconscious and the conscious, between the body and the mind, and the substrate of which is raw emotion.

In accordance with this idea, modern neuroscientists are beginning to consider DMN activity at rest as a spontaneous and free-floating stream of conscious images and thoughts related to memories of past events, predictions of future events, or simply simulating fantasy or virtual scenarios ( Carhart-Harris and Friston, 2010 ;  Schacter et al., 2012 ;  Agnati et al., 2013 ;  Fox et al., 2013 ;  Christoff et al., 2016 ). Some of them denote such a spontaneous function of imagination with the term "mind wandering" ( Mason et al., 2007 ;  Fox et al., 2013 ;  Christoff et al., 2016), which is defined as a tendency to move "higher and more voluminous without a fixed course or a definite goal" ( Simpson, 1989 ). More specifically, mind wandering is a mental exploration characterized by spontaneity, internal orientation, high propensity to explore, positive affect, freedom from constraints, creativity, and high entropy 4 ( Carhart-  Harris  et al., 2014 ;  Christoff et al., 2016 ).

Mind wandering was specifically associated with activity in the medial temporal lobe (MTL), a set of brain regions centered on the hippocampus and parahippocampal complex that constitute the oldest component of the DMN and whose progenitors have been found in the reptilian medial cortex (Reiter et al., 2017 ) . . The MTL is involved in navigating the world (both physically and mentally), as well as in the formation of complex and abstract representations necessary for spatial-temporal orientation, declarative memory, contextual learning, creative thinking, and social behavior (Reiter et al . , 2017 ).

It has been proposed that the MTL is responsible for a self-projective function, whereby a person travels through a virtual dimension imagining what has happened, might happen, or will never happen. Due to this self-projection function, the MTL is involved in the formation of new contextual associations, remembering past episodic events, or mapping virtual, new, and potential future scenarios ( Buckner and Carroll, 2007 ;  Buckner et al., 2008 ;  Buckner, 2010 ;  Maguire and Hassabis, 2011 ).

From our neuroethological perspective, the self-projective exploratory function mediated by the MTL is supported by the expression of a basic emotional drive called the SEARCH disposition, the neural substrates of which are centered on the ascending mesolimbic dopaminergic system and its projection areas of the forebrain (Alcaro et al., 2007; Alcaro and  Panksepp , 2011 ,  2014 ). The SEARCH system is an intrinsic psycho-behavioral function of the brain that has evolved to drive organisms to explore and seek out all kinds of vital stimuli ( Ikemoto and Panksepp, 1999 ;  Alcaro et al., 2007 ;  Alcaro and Panksepp, 2011). Its activation "changes the attitude of a person to the environment, contributing to an increase in appetite, which definitely contributes to the exploration and search for resources, and creates states of expectation that allow animals to predict the availability of future rewards" (Alcaro and Panksepp, 2011, p. 1805 ) .

Interestingly, animal studies have shown that exploratory SEARCH activity is accompanied by the appearance of certain oscillatory rhythms in some areas of the forebrain belonging to the DMN (for a review, see  Alcaro and Panksepp, 2011 ). More specifically, orienting and exploratory movements are associated with theta oscillations in the hippocampal complex, coupled with gamma oscillations in the ventromedial frontal cortex and basal ganglia circuits (for a review, see Alcaro and Panksepp, 2011  ) . These neurodynamic patterns appear to be related to the tendency to move, explore, and approach specific sources of stimulation, as well as to internal attitudes of openness and trust to new and unpredictable experiences ( Alcaro and Panksepp, 2011 ).

In addition, it can also be assumed that the same neurodynamic patterns of SEARCH are also activated when exploratory movements are directed at imaginary objects or thoughts. According to this point of view, studies of patients with brain damage have confirmed that the ventro-medial quadrant of the frontal lobe, rich in SEARCH system projections, is necessary for the generation of dreams and mind wandering (Solms, 1997 , 2000  ) . In addition, the dopaminergic SEARCH system is believed to contribute to creativity, including divergent thinking, cognitive flexibility, innovative insights, and associative thinking ( Flaherty, 2005 ;  Chermahini and Hommel, 2010 ;  Takeuchi et al., 2010 ;  Zabelina et al., 2016; Boot et al., 2017 ).

As noted in the previous article, the research drive to SEARCH can also have an introverted direction, which "allows to direct the SEARCH not only to external objects of perception, but also to internalized mental representations of the subjective environment" (Alcaro and Panksepp, 2011, p . 1809). This phenomenon manifests itself when the categories of stimuli and configurations presented in the MTL do not refer to external space, but instead to an internally oriented psychological world.

As already mentioned in the previous paragraph, self-referential DMN activity at rest is usually restricted to certain orbits or “basins of attraction” ( Carhart-Harris et al., 2014 ;  Christoff et al., 2016 ). In particular, some researchers refer to “automatic constraints” to indicate the involuntary influence of emotion-colored complexes processed in the ventral part of the DMN and SCMS  5  ( Christoff et al., 2016). The expression of automatic emotional constraints can be registered and represented in the MTL, which condenses the operative procedural functioning of the DMN into abstract configurations (image schemas). When such configurations become objects of mind wandering, the unconscious self-referential process processed in the DMN is illuminated and transformed into a noetic conscious flow of mental representations ( Alcaro and Panksepp, 2014 ). As shown in the next paragraph, this function is maximally manifested during dreams in the phase of REM sleep.

DREAMS AS AN ARCHETYPAL FORM OF MIND WANDERING

It has been widely emphasized that REM dreaming is the purest and most archaic phenomenal expression of all possible forms of mind wandering ( Christoff et al., 2016 ). This statement is fully consistent with the psychoanalytic assumption that dreams are the "royal road to the unconscious" because they clearly reveal the functioning of our primary-processual mental activity, which comes from our ancestors (Freud, 1900; Jung , 1967  ) .

Interestingly, recent experimental evidence has shown that reptiles also exhibit neurophysiological and behavioral manifestations of REM sleep ( Shein-Idelson et al., 2010 ). This suggests the possibility that dreaming may not be an exclusive characteristic of humans, but an instinctive function of the vertebrate brain. Interestingly, during REM sleep, endothermic animals lose their capacity for thermoregulation, leaving the body without normal metabolic control, while the brain becomes metabolically hyperactive, especially in certain regions of the medial temporal lobe (MTL) ( Cerri et al., 2017 ). "Regression" to a metabolic pattern similar to an ectothermic state supports the idea that dreaming is an evolutionarily archaic mode of brain-psychic functioning (Panksepp, 1998b ) and they express the primary form of consciousness, or proto-consciousness ( Hobson, 2009 ). This view is also consistent with the fact that the brainstem nuclei that control rapid REM sleep are evolutionarily more ancient than those that control slow-wave sleep and active alertness (Panksepp, 1998b ) . Moreover, neurodevelopmental studies show that REM sleep appears earlier than non-REM and active alertness in human embryos, and that REM sleep mostly predominates in the last trimester of pregnancy , its number gradually decreases after birth ( Birnholz, 1981 ).

The idea that REM dreaming is an archaic and unrestricted form of mind wandering is supported by evidence showing that REM is characterized by:

1. by powerful ("explosive") activation of the mesolimbic dopamine SEARCH system ( Hartman et al., 1980 ;  Gottesman, 1999 ;  Dahan et al., 2007 ;  Perogamvros and Schwartz, 2012 ,  2015 ), which may have something in common with that observed in productive schizophrenia (positive symptoms predominate, such as hallucinations, delusions, states of hypervigilance, etc.;  Solms, 1997 );

2. maximum neurometabolic activity in the MTL, (the oldest part of the DMN, which endotherms share with reptiles) ( Christoff et al., 2016 );

3. high independence of MTL activity from the influence of other areas of the DMN ( Carhart-Harris et al., 2014 );

4. deactivation of areas of the cortex that exercise conscious control over mental functioning and are involved in self-control, such as the orbitofrontal cortex, posterior cingulate cortex, precuneus, dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, and inferior parietal cortex (Nir and Tononi, 2010; Christoff et al  . ., 2016 );

5. deep inhibition of the primary perceptual and motor zones.

Regarding the neurophysiological features of REM sleep, REM dreaming exhibits several phenomenological differences from waking consciousness (for a review, see  Nir and Tononi, 2010 ). First of all, dreams are often characterized by heightened emotional arousal and affective involvement ( Schredl, 2010 ). Such increased emotionality during REM sleep is associated with hyperactivation of the highly emotional limbic brain (for a review, see Nir and Tononi, 2010 ) and, in particular, the SCMS, where all major emotional systems are located ( Panksepp, 1998a , b ;  Alcaro et al., 2017). Interestingly, animals also experience highly emotional experiences in sleep, as cats with lesions of the neural regions responsible for muscle atony during REM sleep exhibit complex emotional behaviors of attack, defense, and exploration ( Sastre and Jouvet, 1979 ). Similarly, highly emotional behavior is also common in subjects with REM sleep behavior disorder who act out their dreams ( Oudiette et al., 2009 ). Moreover, experimental data in humans have shown that after 13 weeks of gestation and in the neonatal period, smiles and other emotional expressions are more frequent, stable, and persistent during REM sleep than during other behavioral states (Dondi et al . al., 2007 ;  Messinger and Fogel, 2007).

Interestingly, from an affective neuroethological perspective, only certain emotional states are overexpressed in dreams, such as surprise, anger, fear, euphoric anticipation (anticipation) (SEARCH), or sexual desire, while sadness, guilt, or depression are rare ( Fosse et al., 2001 ;  Smith et al., 2004 ;  Nir and Tononi, 2010 ). In addition, the stories of dreams of preschool children are often characterized by a clear fulfillment of a wish ( Colace, 2010 ,  2013). This fact supports the original psychoanalytic view that dreaming is often a hallucinatory expression of unfulfilled desire, as well as our neuroethological notion that imagination and dreaming are expressions of the introverted drive to SEARCH. However, we believe that this evidence does not necessarily support the Freudian assumption that dreams serve to relieve excitatory somatic overload (drive reduction theory; see next paragraph).

In adults, dreams are also characterized by reduced attention and volitional control, delusions, disorientation, impaired working memory, and the absence of usual logical and linear spatio-temporal relationships ( Nir and Tononi, 2010 ). In old but apt psychodynamic language, we can say that dream consciousness is characterized by "abbaissament du niveau mental", the collapse of psychological tension, which usually integrates mental contents and organizes them into a rational structure (Janet, 1889 ) .

However, studies of the ontogenesis of dreams have shown that the dreams of preschool children (3–5 years old) are often simple and without strangeness ( Colace et al., 1993 ;  Colace, 2010 ,  2012). These data indicate that startle is not an intrinsic property of neurophysiological events in REM sleep. In our opinion, the increase in the strangeness of dreams during development can be related to the increase in the complexity of the individual brain, and not only due to an attempt to bypass repression, as Sigmund Freud suggests. Indeed, the more the system of noetic memory becomes branched and differentiated, the more those extremely complex synthetic formations that we call images, and which Freud considered to be the product of the condensation process, are revealed in the dream. When a dream does this, it functions as a producer of virtual scenes that express a coherent and symbolic content of our inner world that does not conform to the rules of external reality ( Wilkinson, 2006 ; Alcaro and Panksepp, 2014 ). As Jung suggests, "dreams do not deceive, they do not lie, they do not distort or disguise, but naively announce what they are and what they mean." [...] they invariably seek to express what the Ego does not know and does not understand" ( Jung, 1946 , para. 189).

ABOUT THE FUNCTION OF DREAMS

In an attempt to elucidate the adaptive function that the evolution of dreaming has earned, most studies and theories have focused on the role of dreams in the consolidation of memories (for a review, see  Diekelmann and Born, 2010 ). These theories begin with the phenomenological observation that dreams often involve experiences that the subject had during the day or several days before  Foulkes and Rechtschaffen (1964) ,  Foulkes (1985),  and  Foulkes et al. (1989) . It has also been shown that the amount of time spent in REM sleep during the night after exercise has a positive effect on a person's performance on subsequent days ( Grieser et al., 1972 ;  Barker, 1977).). On the other hand, selective REM sleep deprivation negatively affects performance in the days following exercise ( Chernick, 1972 ). Further experimental studies in humans and animals have shown that during REM sleep there is reactivation of the same neurons and neural circuits that were particularly active during alertness ( Pavlides and Winson, 1989 ;  Maquet et al., 2000 ). Moreover, REM sleep can enhance memory consolidation by activating the expression of genes related to synaptic plasticity ( Ribeiro and Nicolelis, 2004 ;  Rossi, 2004 ).

It has been suggested that dreams participate in the reactivation of brain circuits stimulated during wakefulness, thus strengthening new connections ( Stickgold, 1998 ;  Stickgold et al., 2000 ). Detaching from external work, the organism plunges into an "autonomous" (off-line) psychobiological state, which allows it to come into contact with newly acquired experience, processing and integrating it with the already existing one ( Gais et al., 2000 ; Maquet ,  2001 ;  Mednick et al., 2003 ).

Despite the abundance of evidence, theories of the consolidative function of dreams for memory fail to explain why dreams are not limited to repeating past experiences, but instead rework them in new and creative ways. A careful look at the content of dreams shows that they combine information received the day before with memories of the past, as well as expectations and fantasies (see the next paragraph). As  Ribeiro and Nicolelis (2004) suggest  , dreams "are hyperassociative chains of fragmented memories that model past events and future expectations" (p. 12).

The most influential simulation theory of the function of dreams was proposed by the psychologist and philosopher Revonsuo. He argued that dreaming enables autonomous modeling of threatening events and contributes to the development and maintenance of threat avoidance skills during wakefulness (for a recent review, see  Valli and Revonsuo, 2009 ). It was further shown that such anticipatory autonomic brain training was not aimed exclusively at avoiding negative experiences, but also at approaching desirable situations and conditions (for a review, see  Perogamvros and Schwartz, 2012 ).

PRIMARY AND SECONDARY THINKING PROCESS

The spontaneous and free-floating flow of images that characterize dreams and wandering thoughts has long been recognized in psychoanalysis. Sigmund Freud called it the "primary thought process," describing the process as an instinctive, visual, and unstructured mental processing characteristic of childhood dreams and those suffering from psychosis. If for Freud the primary process is connected only with the discharge of the activity of psychosomatic drives, then for Jung it actually constituted a specific way of "thinking". According to Jung, such processes carry not only an unrecognizable somatic aspect (Freud's drives), not only a fundamental affective content, but also cognitive activity, which can potentially be extremely developed, although it functions in its specific figurative, metaphorical, condensed form.

In accordance with Jungian views, the latest neurodynamic models suggest that dreaming and mind wandering are inferred prognostic processes of the brain, where virtual realities are created in order to optimize the body's expectations and its activity within the external reality ( Hobson and Friston, 2012 ; Moutoussis et al . ,  2014 ). Indeed, experimental evidence has shown that REM sleep improves cognitive flexibility ( Walker et al., 2002 ), decision-making ( Pace-Schott et al., 2012 ), moral reasoning ( Killgore et al., 2007 ), insight ( Wagner et al., 2004 ), and creativity ( Home, 1988 ;  Cai et al., 2009 ; Schacter et al., 2012 ).

It then seems highly plausible that the primary thinking process provides a “mental rehearsal of possibilities”   ( Ribeiro and Nicolelis, 2004 ) that deploys a prospective adaptive function, helping to predict possible future events or find solutions to unresolved problems or conflicts. Such an idea was predicted by Jung, who was in open conflict with Freud, believing that a dream is "a premonition in the unconscious of future conscious achievements, something like a preliminary practice of an outline, or a plan drawn up in advance." Its symbolic meaning sometimes outlines the resolution of the conflict. . .'' ( Jung, 1947/1954 , p. 493).

In modern adults, the primary process of waking life is usually suppressed in favor of a more mature, linear, limited and "rational" way of thinking, which we can call, according to Freud, "secondary thinking process", which is associated with the emergence of a stable sense of self and external reality. Freud and Jung argued that the primary thought process is dominant in all unconscious modes of functioning, in human infancy and in the "savage" man. However, in healthy adults of our modern society, it generally demonstrates a new and more mature form of cognition, characterized by the ability to form metacognition and self-reflection ( Shimamura, 2000 ;  Fleming et al., 2012 ).

It is very difficult to describe what makes this fundamental evolutionary pathway possible, and any attempt to do so is beyond the scope of this publication (see  Carhart-Harris and Friston, 2010 ;  Northoff et al., 2010b ). However, following the neuro-psychoanalytic path, we can consider that in people, attachment relationships become a tool for an additional and fundamental evolutionary step in subjective mental life. This step has been described using the concept of "object cathexis" ( Northoff, 2011 ), that is, with the development from spontaneous imagination focused on affects to imagination focused on the representation of internal objects isomorphic to external perceived objects 6 . This process is directly related to the permanence and stabilization of object representations and self-representation, and allows the transition from the primary to the secondary thinking process ( Carhart-Harris and Friston, 2010 ;  Northoff, 2011 ).

However, the original process does not disappear, but continues to live in the afterlife in the form of unconscious fantasies that affect our mental processes, our moral judgments, our behavior and, everywhere, our conscious activities. Furthermore, the primary thought process may be consciously accessible in unusual states of consciousness such as dreaming, free association, meditation, divergent thinking/creativity, sensory deprivation, near-death experiences, psychedelic states, magical thinking, religious experiences, initial phase of psychosis, and the dream state temporal lobe epilepsy ( Carhart-Harris and Friston, 2010 ;  Carhart-Harris et al., 2014 ).

The archaic function of mind wandering does not disappear in humans, but deploys an important  dispositional  function of rebalancing, which allows maintaining the neuropsychic identity and stability of the organism. Jung called this a "compensatory" function ( Jung, 1935a ,  1956 ). A rather similar hypothesis was put forward by  Jouvet (1975), who considered dreaming to be a form of neurogenetic processing, where new experience is assimilated according to the instinctual psychobiological nature of the organism. According to Jouvet, dreams are a form of neurogenetic programming in which the lived experience of contact with external reality is assimilated and reorganized, starting with one's own psychobiological identity. In our opinion, such a process of constant reprogramming, theorized by Jouvet and Jung, is primarily affective in origin. In fact, if during active alertness emotional dispositions are strongly connected to the conditions of the external environment, then during imagination and dreams they can express themselves more freely, influencing our mental activity in a direct and comprehensive way ( Hopkins, 2016 ).

NEUROEVOLUTION OF IMAGINATION FUNCTION

The evolution of the function of imagination follows a path that can be divided into three stages:

1. Dreaming in the rapid phase of sleep (REM), which is present in reptiles and is the first form of mental imagery.

2. Wandering of thoughts, which is present in endotherms and is an enhancement of the dream-like function of imagination in a state of calm (relaxed) vigor.

3. Limited imagination of the secondary process, which is present in people and is characterized by a stable sense of self and external (physical and social) reality.

REPTILES AND THE DREAMING BRAIN

The evolution of terrestrial vertebrates is characterized by an increase in the complexity and plasticity of their neuro-psycho-behavioral organization and the emergence of a new form of "noetic" consciousness, which is superimposed on the anoetic awareness of the core-Self ( Fabbro et al., 2015 ). Noetic knowledge refers to the experience that "occurs when sophisticated powers of attention enable the specific features of the environment to be clearly distinguished and categorized, which, with sufficient neocortex, allows animals to think ahead." Indeed, when specific aspects of events become the focus of attention, explicit object-reflexive awareness comes to the fore, while semantic (conceptual) memory helps to analyze and categorize the situation ( Tulving, 1985). This is a form of consciousness that Edelman called "primary consciousness" and associated it with the activity of the reverse thalamo-cortical circuits of the brain ( Edelman, 1989 ). Circumstantial evidence suggests that noetic consciousness is present in mammals, birds, and possibly also in reptiles ( Edelman et al., 2005 ), and that human infants born very immature begin to show it after the 3rd month of development" ( Alcaro et al., 2017 , p.10).

As Jung and Jouvet suggest (see previous paragraph), the evolution of noetic knowledge may threaten the maintenance of an integrated and integral psychobiological identity (the Self). Therefore, the processes of focused attention used by terrestrial vertebrates when dealing with the external environment can be compensated by a return to the unrestricted excitation of the "primary process", which turns everything upside down. Thus, the impact of external reality on the organism is accompanied by a reverse movement, thanks to which the organism reorganizes its experience using an instinctive code.

Interestingly, recent evidence has shown that, along with their novel noetic abilities, reptiles exhibit neurophysiological correlates of rapid-onset ( REM) sleep  ( Shein-Idelson et al., 2010 ), suggesting that a primitive form of dreaming evolved in terrestrial vertebrates long before evolution of endothermic species (mammals and birds). This kind of mental nomadism may be related to the autonomous expression of the SEARCH self-projection drive in the medial cortex, which is the reptilian precursor of the mammalian MTL ( Reiter et al., 2017). By creating a vast number of mental images and representations, dream imagery "helps to reinforce the many unconscious habits that are the foundation of our personality." After all, dreams can create powerful subconscious or preconscious affective psychological patterns that make us… the people we are. They can help build many of the emotional myths and beliefs around which our lives revolve" ( Panksepp, 1998b , p. 142). In this important mythopoetic process, dreaming is the first ring of the reflexive function that allows the "Self to become aware" ( Damasio, 2010 ).

ENDOTHERMS AND ENHANCEMENT OF IMAGINATION FUNCTION

Unlike cold-blooded terrestrial vertebrates 7 , mammals and birds have metabolic modifications that facilitate the uptake, transport, and delivery  8  of oxygen (for review, see  Hillenius and Ruben, 2004 ). Due to increased rates of cellular oxygen consumption, mammals and birds are able to maintain higher activity levels for longer periods of time, which has significant ecological benefits. Such endurance allows these animals to forage over large territories and/or make long seasonal migrations in search of favorable resource conditions ( Hillenius and Ruben, 2004 ). 

Increased cardiopulmonary performance not only affects behavioral activity, but also increases the potential resting metabolic rate, which is 10–15 times higher in mammals and birds than in reptiles (Bennett, 1973; Schmidt-Nielsen , 1984  ) . The increased metabolic rate at rest allows you to increase body heat and regulate it autonomously, regardless of the external physical environment ( Hillenius and Ruben, 2004 ). Thus, endothermic species can use excess metabolic capacity to enhance internal order, homeostasis, and their subjective autonomy ( Torday and Miller, 2018 ).

Increased metabolism affects not only body heat, but also the level of brain activity at rest. Although ectotherms show a consistent decrease in brain metabolism when they are not engaged in certain activities, mammals and birds periodically show brain metabolism during sleep and quiescent alertness that reaches and even exceeds the levels characteristic of periods of activity engagement (Mantini et al. , 2011 ;  Lu et al., 2012 ;  Raichle, 2015 ). Thus, endotherms have endogenous self-referential  the processing of the construction of conclusions is not limited, as in reptiles, to short autonomous states of REM-like arousal, but occupies wide stretches of sleep and a calm (relaxed) state of alertness. Thus, they are capable of better research (SEARCH), directed not only to the external world, but also to the inner psychic world. Such an important expansion is associated with the evolution of some ventral and dorsal midbrain cortical structures ( Raichle, 2015 ).

From our neuroethological perspective, the imagination of endotherms is closely related to the evolution of complex socio-affective patterns of relationships, which can be modified by experience and can be constantly reprogrammed within the DMN activity at rest (relaxation). According to this hypothesis, the neuroanatomy of their social emotional systems (PLAY, ANXIETY, and PANIC/SAD) extends to cortico-limbic midline areas belonging to DMN  9  ( Panksepp and Biven, 2012 ). It also seems plausible that in early infancy the attachment process is supported by basic emotional drives and is open to modulation by the imaginative function.

As a consequence of expanding the functions of imagination, endothermic species demonstrate complex social, cognitive and emotional abilities, such as creative thinking, the ability to pretend, some forms of "theory of mind" ("theory of mind"), etc. (Griffin, 1985; Crystal ,  2012 ;  Roberts , 2012 ). In particular, the deductive and innovative aspect of imaginative activity at rest affects communicative skills, enriching them with a repertoire of potentially unlimited signals, signs and symbols 10 . A striking example in this regard is the song of migratory birds such as Sylvia atricapilla ( Portmann, 1986 ), which combines repetition and creative spontaneity, producing a unique and unique language. 

Interestingly, recent data indicate that songbird sleep is characterized by periods of neural activity associated with song reproduction ( Derégnaucourt et al., 2005 ), which plays an important role in vocal learning in songbirds ( Shank and Margoliash, 2009 ;  Margoliash , 2010 ). Although the contribution of different stages of dreaming to such a process has not yet been determined, experimental data show that the plasticity of vocal songs is increased by certain oscillatory bursts of neural activity, which in mammals accompany REM sleep and the activation of the SEARCH brain system (Yanagihara and Hessler, 2012 ) .

MAN AND THE EVOLUTION OF VERBAL THINKING

Originally, the function of imagination was essentially an autonomous process that occurs during REM (reptile) sleep. With the evolution of endotherms, the patterns of the DMN in an energetic resting state are configured in a “transitional space” where internally generated phantasies and external communicative actions overlap. In humans, such a transitional space realizes its enormous potential thanks to the evolution of language communication – a huge leap that separated our species from other animals and which is the basis of all forms of human civilization.

With the mastery of the language, the flow of visual-spatial ideas can acquire the structure of audio-verbal thoughts. Thus, during human development, the collective and transpersonal dimensions can be internalized in the individual psyche, enabling a cultural revolution that is related to and complementary to biological development.

There is an assumption that the evolution of language made it possible to develop "autonoetic consciousness" ( Fabbro et al., 2015 ), which is defined as the ability to represent oneself in space and time, as well as to build a stable and conscious psychological identity, which we can call "Ego". For example, Sigmund Freud emphasized that verbal thinking is the main form of self-conscious perception of inner mental activity. The importance of verbal language and specialized linguistic areas of the brain for self-awareness is now confirmed by influential neuroscientific theories ( Edelman, 1992 ).

However, psychological analyzes of brain-damaged patients show that right-hemisphere damage causes more profound personality disorders and self-awareness deficits than equivalent left-hemisphere lesions, where language areas are located ( Kaplan-Solms and Solms, 2000 ). For example, patients with right-hemisphere damage not only tend to completely ignore their illnesses (anosognosia), but they often have severe psychiatric symptoms, such as profound melancholia, paranoid thoughts, or a complete collapse of ego functions, as seen in productive psychotic states. Kaplan-Solms and Solms (2000) concluded that the right cortico-limbic network is involved in “object cathexis,” a process by which mind wandering is constrained by experience-dependent working attachment patterns organized in the right hemisphere of the brain (Schore, 1994 ) . This type of containment is necessary for the transition from the primary to the secondary thinking process, and then for the stabilization of representations of Ego objects ( Carhart-Harris and Friston, 2010 ;  Northoff, 2011 ).

The method of integration of linguistic and non-linguistic theories of autonoetic consciousness is based on the assumption that verbal thinking allows to move to the secondary process and enhances its functioning only when the subject is able to distinguish the inner world from the outer world. This differentiation involves the mentalization of the experience of separation from the desired/loved object ( Klein, 1921 ;  Bowlby, 1969/1982 ). In our neuroethological perspective, this means the possibility of fully expressing the emotional complex of PANIC/SAD SEPARATION DISTRESS within the limits of conscious self-referential imaginative activity. Only in this case, verbal thinking and mental imagery can be properly attributed to the internal psychological space, which differs from the external social world of others 11 .

We realize that this is a very important topic that needs a deeper and broader analysis, especially considering the process of "object cathexis"  ( Northoff , 2011 ). That is why we propose to devote an entire article to this argument, which will complete the trilogy about the neuro-evolutionary architecture of the human psyche. Such a trilogy began with an article on the affective core of the Self ( Alcaro et al., 2017 ), continued with this article on the function of the imagination of the primary process, and will end with a paper on "object cathexis"  and the secondary process of thinking.

PSYCHODYNAMIC CONSIDERATIONS AND PSYCHOTHERAPEUTIC APPLICATION

In the following pages, we will try to outline our idea of ​​the psychotherapeutic process (which is fundamentally built on the paradigm of analytical psychology) based on the considerations that we have presented in the previous pages. To do this, we must once again recall our vision of the brain-psyche   as a dispositional organic whole that is activated "bottom-up" and "inside-out." This means that the origin and basis of brain-psychic activity  is activated from anoetic anobject affective states through the noetic endogenous production of imagination (schemes of images) to the autonoetic-verbal area. All this is consistent with Jung's idea that the primary level of mental processes is fundamentally affective. This first, pre-formed, affective motivational layer (readiness to respond) acquires a complete psychological form when associated with adequate "images", which actually act as Gestalt modeling structures ( Bucci, 1997). As we have already noted, this connection between affects and images is a fundamental evolutionary achievement (in endothermic animals) and is a prerequisite for healthy mental development in ontogenesis. Indeed, without adequate imaginative containment, the affective states of the "Self" have a diffuse and undifferentiated presence and, therefore, are projected directly onto the external perceptual world. Thus, these vague emotional states and objects of the perceived external world are superimposed on each other. In other words, the individual is not able to "reflect on the experience" and distinguish between the internal and the external.

If, as Jung argued, the unconscious is formed by complexes of ideas (representations) that give form and express fundamental affective dispositions, then the creation of appropriate images means the restructuring of the integrative function to interpret stimuli and then respond to them (that is, experience) according to the nature of the "core Self". Jung described this imagery process in his Red Book ( Jung, 2012 ) or in his essay on the so-called "transcendent function" ( Jung, 1916/1957 ). This process is experienced by artists ( Carta, 2017b ), who are able to first (receptively) find and then (actively) give imaginative form to their sensory states.

For Jung, the two main ways of accessing unconscious entities—what he called "complexes"—were dreams and emergent images activated through "active imagination," both of which have a noetic form of consciousness. Both dreaming and active imagination can ultimately be accompanied by an autonoetic process, by which emerging noetic images can be analyzed and verbalized in an autonoetic (self-reflexively aware) state.

As we have already said, noetic activity is the core of "active imagination" ( Jung, 1916/1957 ,  2012 ), a process analogous to D. Winnicott's concept of non-integration, that is, a relaxed/empty state of the psyche in which the Ego can come into contact with endogenous impulses "True Self"   ( Winnicott, 1964 ,  1965 ). It is also similar to the  well-known  Bollas' concept of the unthinking ( Bollas, 1987 ), which refers to an archaic objectless affective activation that appears in some "regressive" patients as a form of diffuse mood. Khan (1983) also described active imagination in detail as a state in which the mind "lies idle," that is, withdraws from activity in the external world in order to reconnect with the Self. Such a state may be associated with the ability to remain alone, that is, to endure an empty state of rest, open to the perception of internal stimuli (images). This state may also be associated with crucial moments of alert inactivity ( Wolf, 1966 ), during which the infant is likely to rehearse and explore new associative connections through imagery in order to give his experiences emotional stability and cognitive coherence.

From all that we have said up to this point, we are now in a position to outline some fundamental principles of the psychotherapeutic approach, which are based on the process by which the dispositional brain-psyche forms its endogenous images 12 (therefore not necessarily the perceptions or  memories  of perceived biographical events). This means a departure from experiences/images (representations) of external/perceived objects to a state from which endogenous, imaginary images can arise. Therefore, in a certain anti-behavioral way, we will not work on directly overcoming "external" facts, but, first of all, we will look for "internal images", thus rebuilding the ability of the brain-psyche to spontaneously form images. In my own way: learning to daydream correctly within the framework of an analytical situation.

We emphasize the words "first of all", because it is obvious that the connection between the internal and external environment - the brain and the outside world - is circular. What we are trying to say can be expressed through a somewhat strained comparison of the main orientation of behavioral psychotherapy (BT) and a prominent part of cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) with our model. It seems to us that as part of the circular process between disposition and situation — between the brain and the environment — PT and CBT focus their attention on an external object (on the situation). For example, to deal with a phobic response, PT or CBT can systematically intervene in how the subject behaves with the perceived object, trying to better respond and adapt it to the situation. If the phobic object is a mouse or an elevator, the psychotherapist will focus on them.

Our goal is probably the same — to ensure a more harmonious adaptation to the environment. However, we would regard the perceived object that has become so significant to the phobic patient not as a real object to which the patient is drawn, but as a "fetish" that contains or refers to another, unconscious self-referential process that the subject is not yet able to imagine due to a number of reasons. Such an unconscious process is activated by an anoetic affect, which is projected onto an external object (phobia, fetish)  in search of an imaginary form  13. In a "normal" situation, external objects can give fairly good imaginative form to the subject's anoetic objectless affects, so that we are usually unaware of the fact that, in fact, every significant (affectively significant) connection represents the emergence of an internal, endogenous, potential affective image. As Winnicott explains, the form that the external object gives to the endogenous affect must be "just" good enough to promote and maintain the activity of the SEARCH system at a productive level and, therefore, to promote creative adaptation  14. Our work will therefore be directed not at the external—perceived—object, but at the autonomous image which we expect to rise from the unconscious patient through the anxious, phobic tone of feeling which the fetish contains. Again, the process is actually circular, but our approach is not to adapt the subject to his situation, but to help him dispositionally interpret it as something that is both "found and created" (Winnicott, 1967) — that is, to transform facts into values. To do this, we must help the patient to look at his objects as projected images that are symptomatic and not symbolic in nature, since they are not yet able to express the dispositional affective nature of the patient in his environment. In other words, such object relations express the inability not only to adapt, but to adapt to the world in a personalized (creative) way, because, as Heidegger would say, people do not live in the environment, but in the  world .

In general, we usually interpret anxiety not as a non-specific reaction to an environmental threat, but as an inability of the brain to form "good images", that is, images that, following Jung's model, can give an imaginary noetic form to the affects that organize the Self into complexes and, ultimately, rest, in autonomous, verbal activity. Thus, we believe that psychotherapeutic efforts aimed directly at the external objective world, especially through higher cognitive functions, actually point to a secondary level—the response/adaptation level. And our model, on the contrary, aims to focus on the primary level, which deals with the inability of the brain to form good images, that is, to correctly organize its own ascending activity, from basic unprocessed affects to self-reflective consciousness,

From our point of view, the spontaneous formation of conscious sensory-colored images is itself therapeutic because it creates conditions in which rejected   affects (this rejection can reach the level of what  Lacan, (1958)  called "exclusion") can gain access to the realm of imagination, and then, ultimately, can be perceived by self-consciousness. This is possible due to the creation of conditions for a possible regression to a state of relaxation or non-integration, which promotes spontaneous imagination. Such a regressive state is associated with a "regressive" transition from an autonoetic (verbal) state of consciousness to a noetic, pre-reflective awareness. Jung (1946) has long theorized this state, but Freud also had it in mind when he wrote about the regressive state of the patient lying on the couch, in which the patient can "freely" associate, while the analyst maintains a state of free-floating attention. Both authors clearly indicate the fundamental condition of psychotherapy: not only the patient, but also the psychotherapist must maintain a noetic level of consciousness.

Being fundamentally affective, the endogenous images of the patient and the analyst will be representations of their inter-affective analytic field: their psychological  meeting place  (see  Stern, 1985 ;  Sander, 2007 ) which is often described as  transference. The creation of this transference field is made possible by the analyst's tendency to affectively/figuratively attune to the patient. At the same time, the analyst must be open to any signal that belongs to what Jung, based on his theory of (psychosomatic) complexes, called somatic transference. In this state, not only verbal meanings are important. In fact, the central place is occupied by expressions that are based on implicit meanings and that can be conveyed by non-verbal manifestations or concrete actions  15 . At the same time, the therapeutic relationship can gradually develop into a sophisticated form of mutual active imagination, in which the analytic couple forms a network of relationships formed by the combination of their conscious and unconscious processes (for a description of this situation, in which the analytic relationship is also a process of joint imagination, see (Jung, 1935b  16 ).

It is very important that within the transference field the analyst immerses himself in a dual mental state in which he is at the same time self-reflective and in touch with his inner noetic (dreamlike) activity. The therapist must be able to master a dual state of consciousness—a noetic form of imagination controlled by autonoetic activity—a hybrid state necessary to perform what  Bion (1985)  called  rêverie  17 . This means that he will be attuned to the level of psychological formal activation of the patient, so that the images that arise in the therapist's mind, which have been inter-affectively attuned to the patient, may in fact be (also) the same. One of us discussed this situation and called it “ecstatic” ( Carta, 2017a). It happens that in this state, images also flow through the psyche of the analyst. These should be approached carefully and critically, as they may be aspects of the patient's anoetic (purely bodily/affective) states that are projected onto the analyst or that the analyst simply experiences. The same can be said about the elements of the analyst's somatic transference.

Due to the dual state of his mental activity (noetic and autonoetic), the therapist has the opportunity to verbally reflect on these images in an autonoetic conscious state. For the patient, this means being able to express the noetic images that arise and discuss them with the analyst—an extremely important function indeed. If the image is a reflection of one's affective state, then the autonoetic word is a self-reflection of the image. However, during the noetic phase of imagining, the verbal nature of the treatment must be suspended. Let the images flow first, and then talk about them.

We would like to emphasize that the transformation from image to word occurs gradually. In fact, before words can fully express self-reflection and a mature relation to the object (without confusion or division between self and object), the quality of language is profoundly metaphorical. In essence, metaphors are verbal formations closest to images, since they tend to condense two terms into a hybrid third, which, like an image, can be understood through synthetic perception,  en masse . At this point in the process, non-metaphorical, intellectual, analytic words can seem inappropriate, abstract, or worse, dissociating because of their intellectual/intellectualizing nature. In our experience, metaphorical language is the most common form of language that flows between patient and analyst. This is not yet fully realized (autonoetic) language, but it is a direct verbal direct product of the transformation of affective content, expressed or such that can be expressed with the help of images (dreams).

In summary, the functions of a psychotherapist and the therapeutic process can be summarized as follows :

1.  Description.  The psychotherapist helps the patient to describe his situation. He will not try to interfere with the patient's idea of ​​his external objects, but will help the patient to focus on feelings and on any associations that may arise from this idea. If, for example, the patient refers to his phobia of flying, at this stage the psychotherapist will not suggest any interventions or any possible modifications, because the image of flying may not actually be the best image associated with the original affect, but only a second the associative carrier of this particular affect. We believe that from this stage the language spoken is already deeply metaphorical. Intellectualization, theorizing or sharp analysis are now counterproductive. The psychotherapist should already be in a dual state of consciousness .

2.  Concentration .  The patient sinks into a state of rest (relaxation).  At this stage, the psychotherapist helps the patient move away from his involvement in external images/objects and focus on feelings. In fact, in order for an endogenous image to rise, it is necessary for the patient to focus intently on the tone of feeling or the specific mood that is the  prima materia from which the images are formed. Such concentration should also be aimed at bodily sensations, tension, any movements, while ensuring proper breathing  18 . While the patient sinks into a state of relaxation and begins to imagine, he should not talk. Verbal activation and speech can now interfere with the process of imagination . 

3.  Containment.  The psychotherapist must contain the patient when the patient feels contact with endogenous images. This means being able to go where the patient is, which we believe is the cornerstone of psychotherapy. This process involves mature and sustained empathy and the ability to somehow see  who  the patient is and what emotional/imaginary environment he/she is actually in. Such a containing function is also important because the enteroceptive and proprioceptive experience of the patient can be changed in a certain way during the appearance of emotionally colored endogenous images. His body schema and body image  ( Dolto, 1984 ),  therefore, feelings of his subjectivity can be perceived as, for example, as if the patient is, to some extent, a child.

4.  Discussion.  The psychotherapist should help the patient interact and discuss his endogenous images. During this stage, the patient is still limited in his verbal  interactions , to only briefly communicate, if necessary, what is happening between him and his images .

5.  Working out.  At this stage, the patient can share his experience of working with endogenous sensory-colored images and interactions. During such a phase, the endogenous images will be consistent with the patient's "real" objective life experience reported during phase (1), as well as with associations with subsequent objective life experience and past memories. Here, the psychotherapist's experience in mastering his own dual state of consciousness will be the main tool for the transition between these two realities, and the transfer of the emotional-cognitive-relational content of autonomous images into the patient's life. At this stage, the language used can become less metaphorical, more analytical and, in some ways, abstract, as it can autonoetically express and shed light on relationships and connections.

Before presenting a very brief example of this process, we can explain that its ascending nature does not really describe it in a fair way. In fact, the psychotherapeutic process that we describe begins "from below"—affects and somatic activation—through images, through metaphors, to autonoetic cognitive content, but by its continuous nature it actually describes a cycle in which we find the necessary space for an upward flow from of cognitive cognition to affects, as in phase (5). However, we want to point out again that this last (autonoetic, verbal) phase may not actually occur, and that the reintegration between the affective/motivational/intentional layer and the patient's experience with his relational reality may be implicit. In fact, we think that this cycle is constantly happening implicitly in every moment of life. Our description,.

CLINICAL Essay

A very brief clinical outline follows. Our goal is not to discuss a clinical case (since this sketch is not even a clinical vignette), but to show a possible way to apply what we have discussed so far in the clinical field .

The patient is a 40-year-old unmarried man. He experiences a period of pervasive dysthymic mood in which he is overwhelmed by feelings of uselessness, despair, and passivity. He is stuck in his world of relationships (both in his relationship with his girlfriend, which he cannot turn into a stable relationship that would turn him into a father, and in his relationships with colleagues) and in his professional/creative sphere, because he does not feel like able to be intelligent and productive. During the previous session, he felt like he was in some kind of swamp (figuratively). He described, again, what happened between sessions in connection with this depressed feeling (DESCRIPTION) .

At the end of the hour, he suddenly remembered an old dream. The therapist told him that they could return to this dream in the next session, which we are now describing .

P. I looked in my notes for a description of the dream that occurred at the end of the last session, but I could not find it. Nevertheless, I remember the facade of a small church in the Baroque style, made of light stone. I enter through the door and find myself in a large cave, it is like a womb, dark and full of blood. I am delighted with this image. There is a naked woman, crucified on two large, rough trunks of trees, thinned only where there are branches, which hang high near one of the walls. A witch is flying above me, and I am tied to a large trunk lying on the ground, on one side by my legs, and on the other by my arms, which are stretched above my head. I am naked, and the witch suddenly attacks me, in pique, and the crucified woman dangles, crying and screaming in pain .

I had this dream years ago and it makes me think about how much time has passed and how these themes are still so alive in me. What do you think has changed over the years?

T. I think that in some things you have changed, and in others you have changed less. You still don't dedicate yourself to being involved in the world, and you don't fully dedicate yourself to your relationships with others, especially in the case of your girlfriend .

A few minutes of silence.

Q. I have been feeling very tired for the past few days. I have a hard time getting up in the morning and I have no energy. I have a workshop to do on Saturday and the thought of it is depressing me. I feel very tired these days. It is difficult to get up in the morning. I have no energy. I have a seminar to teach on Saturday and the thought is depressing me. APPENDIX 1. FORMATION OF SCHEMES OF IMAGES

In cognitive psychology and neuroscience, images are (re)representational formations of structural configurations, which are analogous to what are called schemas ( Sheets-Johnstone, 2010 ;  Cappuccio and Wheeler, 2012 ), or maps  20  ( Damasio, 1999 ,  2010 ), and, that have a more fascinating and pervasive meaning, Jung called them complexes.

According to  Mandler (1988 ,  1992 ,  2000) , image schemes arise from the comparison of stimuli and are the basis for the formation of abstract concepts. Mendler refers to perceptual analysis as the fundamental procedure by which, using the external perception obtained by means of what she calls perceptual recognition, the brain-psyche (brainmind) expresses on the psychic level the innermost symbolic meaning of its life. For Mandler, perceptual recognition is a sensorimotor activity that occurs automatically and does not require any conceptual basis. On the other hand, perceptual analysis is an active process of comparison between stimuli, which is the earliest evidence of a contemplative attitude ( Knox, 2004a) and forms the basis of the formation of abstract concepts.

Like  Knox (2001 ,  2004a , b) , we consider the mechanism of perceptual analysis described by Mandler as a compelling model that can explain how mental images are formed. One fundamental point is that schematic representations are not created by "perceptual recognition." In fact, it is an active, innate process of perceptual analysis that uses perceptual recognition to form a re-presentation of the internal properties of the living organism (instincts, drives, emotions). In this sense, image schemas can be identified with Jungian archetypes as such, and their patterned representational content can be attributed to what Jung described as "the self-reflection of the instinct towards itself"   ( Jung, 1948 , p. 257).

Here we disagree with  Fonagy (2002) , for whom early mental structures are produced (not changed) by infants' early preverbal experiences before consciousness emerges. Thus, due to this situational bias or interaction with the Self's environment, any psychological image can be perceived as innate for Fonagy, when in fact it is not. Our dispositional bias also contrasts with the views shared by  Knox (2004a) ; Saunders and Skar (2001) or Merchant, who, inverting Jung's psychogenetic theory, see archetypal images as arising from psyche/brain structures (image schemas) that were laid down in early infant life as a result of the infant's developmental experiences (mainly interpreted through attachment theory) , so that although the complexes arise first, "archetypes" will be the label resulting from the further categorization of these archaic experiences.

According to the Jungian perspective, we must emphasize that the isomorphism between image-schemas and the objective world to which they belong does not mean that self-produced mental images resemble external images. Our starting point for the dispositional bias is our extraordinary ability, based on the human race's "knowledge" of natural numbers, to "know" "external reality." In fact, the reflexive isomorphism we are talking about is based on the radically "transitive" (Winnicottian) nature of the natural numbers, which are both invented by the mind and revealed in the external world. Therefore, the schemes of images we are talking about are "means" (rooted in perceptual structures, for example, visual, but not only), reaching through the process of abstract refinement of its final mathematical (Jung, 1935a ; von Franz, 1974 ). This connection between the brain/psyche's endogenous ``knowability'' of the object world and mathematical (scientific) knowledge [as in some revelatory dreams, such as Descartes's famous dream (Von Franz, 1998) ] is basis of Hintergrudphysik of  Pauli (2006) .

In general, we disagree with the view emerging from attachment theory because it interprets psychogenetic development through what we call a situational bias. In fact, the novel dispositional scaffolding process we mentioned earlier, which metaphorizes higher concepts based on perceptual-motor input, is not primarily shaped by early interactions, but actually uses meaningful interactions to shape primary endogenous affects into complex emotions. The key to this dispositional process is that perceptual analysis (which, as we have seen in the previous pages, is an active endogenous pattern-forming activity involving the DMS) is not primarily activated by exteroperceptions, but by the emotional SEARCH system, initially activated in connection with enteroceptive stimuli. Damasio, 2010 ). In fact, it is an affective scheme of images that carries emotional-cognitive content, which then gives value to perception and, therefore, precedes external perception.
