The Affective Core of the Self: A Neuroarchetypal Perspective on the Foundations of Human (and Animal) Subjectivity

Translated by Anna Stembkovska

Professional editing by Dmytro Zalesky, Iryna Semkiv

Antonio Alcaro 1,2 ,   Stefano Carta 3  і   Jaak Panksepp 4

Front. Psychol., 01 September 2017 | https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2017.01424

Psychologists usually considered "Self" as an object of experience that appears when an individual perceives it in the field of consciousness. According to this view, the ability of the human psyche to self-present is associated with corticolimbic learning processes that occur during individual development. On the other hand, Carl Gustav Jung viewed the Self as the core of our personality, in its conscious and unconscious aspects, as well as in its actual and potential forms. According to Jung, the Self derives from an innate dynamic structure that integrates the meaningful drives of our "brain-mind" and leads to both instinctive behavioral actions and archetypal psychological experiences. Interestingly, recent neuroethological research indicates that our subjective identity is based on archaic neuropsychic processes, which humans have in common with other animals and are part of their innate constitutional repertoire. Indeed, the brain activity in the subcortical structures of the midbrain (PCSS) is intrinsically related to the emergence of prototypical affective states, which not only flexibly influence our behavior, but also being the source of specific experiences and moods (which are the first form of self-orientation in the world) change the field our consciousness. Moreover, such affective dynamics play a central role in the organization of individuality and in the evolution of all other (more complex) psychological functions. Thus, based on the convergence of the most modern scientific research and some psychological intuitions of Jung, we intend to explore here the first neuroevolutionary layer of the human mind, which we call the Affective Core of the Self.

"I have long thought that, if there is any analogy between mental and physiological processes, the organizing system of the brain must lie in the subcortex in the brainstem. This assumption arises from considerations of the psychology of the [Self] archetype, which is of central importance and universal distribution represented in the symbols of the mandala. The reason that led me to suggest that the physiological basis of this archetype is localized in the brainstem was the psychological fact that the unifying properties of this archetype are mostly affective, besides, it has an organizing and orienting value. I would suggest that such a subcortical system may somehow reflect a characteristic of an archetypal form of the unconscious." Jung (1958 , para. 582).

Introduction

In the Western philosophical and scientific tradition, mental subjective life, which arises as a result of the activity of highly developed and complex neocortical cognitive functions, is generally considered as the prerogative of human beings. These views are supported by the popular neurocognitive paradigm, which is still used to explain the localization of subjective experiences in higher limbic and cortical areas of the brain ( LeDoux, 1996 ;  Rolls, 1999 ;  Kandel, 2005 ), promoting the belief that consciousness arises only from within thalamocortical networks ( Edelman, 1989 ;  Crick, 1994). In accordance with such views, psychoanalysts and cognitive psychologists believed that subjectivity is acquired during the individual development of a person and departs from the introjection of some attachment-related working models, which leads to a kind of reflexive self-perception (Stern, 1985 ;  Schore , 1994 ;  Fonagy, 2001 ).

A compelling version of such corticocognitive anthropocentrism has recently been tested in several publications by Joseph LeDoux ( LeDoux, 2015 ;  LeDoux and Brown, 2017 ). He considers the subjective experience of a person as the result of higher-order cortical processes involved in cognitive interpretations of our existence in the world. The limitation of this approach stems from the misconception that subjective experience is necessarily related to the fact of self-awareness of this experience, as this can be verified by asking a person "what do you feel?". Conversely, many different researchers in the field of psychology, philosophy and neuroscience have recognized the existence of subjective experiences without self-awareness, which appears to be only a secondary evolutionary product of our mental life ( James, 1890/1950; Edelman, 1992 ; Searle, 1992 ; Damasio, 2010 ).

In accordance with the empirical point of view  of James (1890/1950 ), the neurophilosopher Georg Northoff defined the "phenomenal minimal Self" as a pre-reflective form of subjectivity. It includes an experience that is defined by certain (pre)conscious qualities, or " qualia ", and an implicit sense of belonging to that experience ( Northoff, 2013 ;  Northoff et al., 2014 ). The advantage of such a definition is to pay attention to two necessary and sufficient characteristics of the Self: intentionality (intentionality) and conscious sensitivity (sensitivity) ( McGinn, 1989 ;  Searle, 1991). Intentionality as a goal-directed organizing process can be equated to describing the Self as a dynamic pattern unfolding toward a goal. Consciousness is a process by which the flow of dynamic changes permeating the Self is accompanied by qualitative tangible experiences.

Empirical data about a person indicate that the "minimum Self" is associated with the creation of mental images and the processing of representations (images) that emerge as a result of the "resting activity" of the CSC (cortical internal (middle) structures) ( Qin and Northoff , 2011 ;  Musholt, 2013 ;  Northoff et al., 2014 ;  Hu et al., 2016 ;  Weiler et al., 2016 ;  LeDoux and Brown, 2017 ). Moreover, the CSC is anatomically and functionally closely related to a set of PCSCs (midbrain subcortical structures) ( Northoff and Panksepp, 2008 ;  Panksepp and Northoff, 2009), which are considered the neurophysiological substrate of the form, the primordial form of subjectivity, called the "Nuclear Self" by members of this research group ( Panksepp, 1998b ) and the "proto-Self"  Damasio (1999) . Interestingly, the PSC is the most emotional part of our brain (perhaps even more so than the overrated limbic system), and the affective neurodynamics that occur in the PSC play a central role in the organization of human personality (Panksepp, 1998b, 2007; Davis et al . ,  2003 )  . ;  Northoff et al., 2006 ;  Davis and Panksepp, 2011 ;  Panksepp and Davis, 2014 ;  Montag et al., 2016). Therefore, as a direct expression of internal forms of functioning - "intentions in action" ( Panksepp, 1998b ) - affects can be considered as basic organizers of the Self ( Panksepp, 1998a , b ,  2005 ,  2010 ,  2011 ;  Damasio, 1999 ;  Denton, 2006 ), and express a form of rudimentary consciousness (or proto-consciousness), which is characterized by primary processes and extra-objective (anoetic) feelings ( Alcaro and Panksepp, 2014 ).

At the beginning of this article, we quoted Jung's text, which contains three more or less directly expressed main theses:

(1) Among all the other Jungian archetypes 1  there is one (Self) which is central and universally distributed. It is represented in highly organized geometric (and symbolic) "mandalic" patterns ( Jung, 1933/1950 ,  1951 ) (Figure 1).

(2) If there is some correspondence between brain and psyche, then the physiological basis of the Self can be located in the brainstem, which is the fundamental organizing system of the entire brain and psyche.

(3) The unifying properties of the Self are primarily affective, apart from the role of ordering and orientation.

Accordingly, we think that such statements of Jung were not only far-sighted, they actually open up ways of interrelationship of his theory of the psyche with the latest scientific theories and discoveries of modern times.

The subcortical nucleus of the Self

The hemispheres of the human brain are connected to the spinal cord and the rest of the body through a set of PCSS. The functional anatomy of the ACL approaches maturity at birth (Figure 2). In his seminal neuroevolutionary work,  MacLean (1990) refers to the PCSS as the "reptilian brain" because they are already present in reptiles and are conserved quite homologously (but not completely unchanged) in the form of layering from birds to mammals. Although this metaphor has received much criticism, we assume that it means a homologously widespread, (but with species-specific variations) across all vertebrates "primary process).

Studies in humans and animals show that lesions of the PCSS cause cerebral coma and the cessation of any form of mental and intentional life: all mental activity collapses and organisms become zombie-like, exhibiting a predominantly vegetative existence without intentionality (Panksepp, 1998a , b ;  Watt and Pincus, 2004 ;  Merker, 2007 ;  Panksepp and Biven, 2012). Moreover, the deficit becomes more severe the lower down the neuro-axis the damage occurs. For example, experimental animal studies indicate that complete destruction of the periconductive gray matter (PAG), which underlies PCSS, leads to a complete destruction of the processing of environmental events related to the self. With complete damage to the PAG, all activity directed at the world is impaired. Animals are in a state of partial alertness, but they do not seem to be able to clearly perceive things ( Panksepp and Biven, 2012 , p. 409).

On the other hand, significant lesions of the cortex of the hemispheres and the upper limbic (peripheral) lobes do not destroy the field of consciousness. For example, animals deprived of their cortex early in life maintained remarkable levels of behavioral coherence, intentionality, and spontaneity. Not only do they show the ability to learn from events with positive or negative reinforcement, but they actually show more emotionality than animals with an intact brain ( Huston and Borbely, 1973 ,  1974 ).

The relevant evidence base in humans is based on a condition called hydrocephalus, in which the cerebral cortex and higher limbic regions are completely destroyed in utero, while most of the subcortical networks remain functional. Surprisingly, such children with hydrocephalus show many signs of positive and negative affective states. For example, they:

“[…] express pleasure with a smile and a laugh, and immediately with a “fussy” arching of the back and crying (in many gradations, these emotional states are depicted on their faces). A familiar adult can use this sensitivity to build play sequences that predictably progress from smiling, to giggling, laughing, and intense child excitement" ( Merker, 2007 , p. 79).

Moreover, they also exhibit certain forms of affective learning and memory. They:

“[…] implement behavioral initiatives within the strict limits of their motor disability in the form of instrumental behavior, for example, create noise by kicking rattles hanging in a special frame designed for this purpose (the “small room”), or activate their favorite toys with keys - probably on the basis of associative learning of the connection between actions and their consequences. Such behavior is accompanied by signs of satisfaction and excitement on the part of the child corresponding to the situation" ( Merker, 2007 ).

In conclusion, evidence from animal and human studies demonstrates that the primary form of the Self originates within the PSC, and that all other complex forms of mental life may depend on these brain regions, as damage or disruption of the PSC causes the collapse of all conscious and/or intentional activity  . . Despite the fact that a member of this research group called this structure the "core Self" ( Panksepp, 1998b ), and  Damasio (1999)  called it the "proto-Self", here we would like to adopt the definition " Affective Core  Self "", to emphasize the absolute relevance of the affective dimension. Indeed, as a consequence of its internal neurophysiology and neuroanatomical organization, the PCSS is involved in the emergence of basic affective states, which differentiate within three categories ( Panksepp and Biven, 2012 ):

(1) Homeostatic/visceral affects refer to internal bodily states that are perceived at the conscious level as general moods and feelings. Indeed, PKSS receive information directly from the body and, in turn, regulate visceral and somatic states, controlling the endocrine, exocrine, and autonomic nervous systems  2  ( Damasio, 1996 ,  1999 ;  Porges, 2011 ). Because of the tight connection with the "internal environment" of the body ( Bernard, 1865/1961 ), the PCS is also called the "visceral brain" ( MacLean, 1990 ).

(2) Instinctive/emotional affects refer to internal and most important dispositions to act, approach, or avoid certain situations (see next paragraph). Indeed, the SCSs are already evolutionarily equipped with neural systems that trigger and control instinctive patterns of action and postures necessary for the survival and reproduction of the organism (e.g., locomotor and orientational movements, sexual behavior, ingestive behavior, etc.) ( Tinbergen, 1951 ; Lorenz ,  1965 ;  Panksepp, 1998b ;  Denton, 2006 ;  Arminjon et al., 2010 ). Some of these instinctive patterns consist of basic emotional dispositions such as fear, anger, or joy, etc. ( Panksepp, 1998b ) (see next paragraph).

(3) Sensory affects refer to rudimentary perceptual experiences with intrinsic affective value, such as the sense of touch, the ability to hear soothing or disturbing sounds, the sensation of heat or cold, sweet or bitter tastes, etc. Indeed, the PCSS are provided by some primary sensory intermediate stations for exteroceptive and proprioceptive perception ( Merker, 2007 ). Interestingly, such subcortical perceptual areas are closely related to neural motor nuclei responsible for directing attention, such as the superior tuberosity and/or the optic roof, which control eye movements ( Stein et al., 2002 ).

Emotional affects

Studies of the brain with electrical stimulation 3  made it possible to localize various operational systems of the mammalian brain, the activation of which is responsible for the appearance of at least seven main emotional dispositions ( Panksepp, 1998a , b ,  2005 ,  2010 ;  Panksepp and Biven, 2012 ): SEARCH systems, ANGER systems , FEAR systems, LUST systems, CARE systems, PANIC/SEPARATION GRIEF systems, and PLAY systems (Figure 3). Four of these emotional systems have also been found in the reptilian brain, although they may have lacked (or are underdeveloped) certain recently evolved social emotions (CARING, PANIC/GRIEF, and PLAY).

Emotional operating systems are instinctive neural devices that produce a series of coordinated and integrated responses that form a characteristic repertoire of emotions 4 . Moreover, the data obtained in the study of animals and humans convincingly indicate that the activation of each emotional system changes the subjective state of the organism and leads to the emergence of characteristic feelings that are perceived on a conscious or experiential level. Indeed, people describe strong emotional feelings during electrical stimulation of the PCSS ( Heath, 1964 ,  1996 ); whereas in animals such stimulation always produces reward or aversion effects that are likely to influence their future behavior ( Panksepp, 2010 ,  2015 ).

The (pre)conscious characteristics of emotions are intrinsically related to the nature of their intention, since positive and negative affective feelings always reflect the intention to approach or avoid certain situations, which allows us to define emotions as primary forms of intention dispositions. For example, the disposition of SEARCH prompts the organism to search for basic resources (food, water, sex, etc.), FEAR - to avoid the source of danger, etc. 5. Therefore, emotions always express a dispositional-intentional nature, adjusting the organism to the privileges of the world, thus determining the basic affective attitudes of animals and (in conjunction with cortical networks) the areas of use of behavioral strategies. Such a teleological point of view is also fundamental for Jung's model of the psyche. She considers emotional affects as self-perception of internal patterns of functioning - "intentions in action" ( Panksepp, 1998b ) - which expresses a form of "anoetic" consciousness, which is the first primary layer of the brain, in which the nuclear Self affectively experiences its own sense of self 6 .

According to recent nonlinear dynamic theories ( Freeman, 1999 ;  Brown, 2002 ;  Llinas, 2002 ), emotional affects can be more adequately identified as neurodynamic patterns that arise within the PCSS and then spread throughout the brain and body, thus enticing the organism to activity. and pushing it to specific “sources” of neuronal activation (see also  Panksepp, 2000 ;  Krieger, 2014). Thus, emotional affects act as vectors that orient an ensemble of behavioral and mental activity in the direction of the orbit of values. When they are transmitted to the spinal cord and other motor or visceral effector systems, they become integrated ensembles of physiological and behavioral instinctual responses. Spreading to higher areas of the brain, they can take the form of ensembles of mental representations (archetypal images or thoughts) that gravitate to a characteristic affective core (Figure 4).

When neurodynamic patterns develop in a rigid and automatic manner, they generate stereotyped behavioral or mental actions characterized by compulsion and affect the subjective world as external factors or, using Jungian terminology, as "ectopsychic factors":

"Among the psychological factors that determine human behavior, instincts are the main forces driving mental events. […] If we consider the appearance of the psyche as a relatively recent event in the history of evolution, and assume that the mental function is a phenomenon accompanying the nervous system, which in one way or another has become centralized, it would be difficult to believe that from the very beginning the instincts had mental character. And since the connection of the psyche with the brain is a more likely assumption than the psychic nature of life in general, I consider the characteristic of   instinct compulsion to be an ectopsychic 7  factor. Nevertheless, it is psychologically important because it leads to the formation of structures or patterns that can be considered as determining factors of human behavior" ( Jung, 1937/1942, p. 234).

However, emotional dispositions differ from rigid behavioral automatisms because the ensemble of actions coordinated by each emotional system is flexibly organized and modulated. As a result, emotions are interpreted as "flexible patterns of action" ( Llinas, 2002 ), which respond to trigger stimuli, but also  predict  future events,  prepare  the organism to cope with uncertain situations, and, with the preservation of the neocortex,  orient  its attitudes according to specific intended ways. The inner non-deterministic quality of emotional dispositional patterns opens the material/organic structure for the entry of the mental intentional/conscious embryo ( Bergson, 1896/1991 ). As Jung emphasized:

"[…] The direct determining factor is not the ectoplastic instinct, but the structure that arises as a result of the interaction of the instinct and the current mental situation. Thus, the determining factor will be  the modified  instinct. The changes that instinct undergoes are as significant as the difference between the color we see and the objective wavelength that produces it. Instinct as an ectopsychic factor would simply play the role of a stimulus, while instinct as a psychic phenomenon would represent the assimilation of this stimulus into an already existing pattern. This process should be named. I would call it the term " psychization" 8 " ( Jung, 1937/1942 , p. 234).

Basic affects as archetypal "psychoid" states and two-aspect monism

At the current historical moment, scientific research cannot explain how the neurodynamic activity of the PCSS is related to the emergence of basic affective states that are experienced at the subjective level (consciousness) and that can teleologically influence the course of material events (intentionality) (Jonas, 1976 ;  Searle, 1992 ). This difficulty probably arises when subjective states (first-person experiences) are reductionistically reduced to objective material processes (third-person events) in a mistaken attempt to place an intense and non-localized phenomenon within a three-dimensional spatial field (Bergson, 1896 /1991 ).

A very influential empirically oriented philosophical tradition considers affective feeling as a phenomenon intrinsically connected with the perception of internal visceral and bodily states, a kind of integrated enteroception. The most famous theory in this approach was proposed independently  by James (1884)  and  Lange (1887) . They assumed that affective feelings reflect a cortical-cognitive representation of peripheral-unconscious arousal activated in the body by certain instinctive/emotional devices. Recently, Antonio Damasio proposed a new version of the James-Lange theory, correcting its initial corticocentrism and suggesting that the PCSS is involved in the first stage of the representation of somatic and visceral states ( Damasio, 1996 ,  1999 ,  2010).

Damasio's concept has bright predecessors in the history of psychoanalysis. In fact, regarding affects as states of visceral tension and relaxation, Freud wrote:

"The id, cut off from the outside world, has its own world of perception. It detects with extraordinary acuteness certain changes within, especially fluctuations in the intensity of its instinctual needs, and these changes become conscious as feelings in the series of pleasure-dissatisfaction" ( Freud, 1940/1964 , p. 190).

Recently, the Freudian visceral views have been picked up again by neuropsychoanalysts, who postulate a strict correspondence between the internal physiological environment and the mental world of individuals ( Solms and Turnbull, 2002 ). According to this view, affective feelings are related to the process of homeostasis ( Cannon, 1929 ;  Denton, 2006 ) and then to the messages the body sends to the brain-mind about survival and reproductive success or failure. Moreover, recent developments in the neuropsychoanalytic homeostatic approach also suggest that embodied interaction with other people in childhood allows one to “mentalize” basic visceral sensations, transforming gross internal perception into subjective feelings ( Fotopoulou and Tsakiris, 2017 ) .

However, the physiological connection of the PCSS with the internal and external environment of the body does not mean that affective feelings simply derive from neurocognitive representations of visceral and somatic functions: for example, when reflecting on the nature of affects, Freud himself noted that he was skeptical that:

"This list allowed us to get to the essence of the affect. It seems that in the case of some affects, we look deeper and realize that the main […] is  the repetition of a certain special meaningful experience . This experience could only be  a very early impression of a very general character, placed in the prehistory not of an individual, but of a species"  (Freud, [28], p. 395).

In this highly significant passage, Freud abandons his traditional reductionist perspective in favor of a Lamarckian and anti-reductionist approach 9 . According to him, the inner core of affect is not just a somatic material process, but a psychic event ("a very early impression of a very general nature, placed in the prehistory not of an individual person, but of a species"). Therefore, the reductionist position should not exclude the broader, non-reductionist paradigm that Jung constantly developed in his writings. Perhaps the best example of such a double vision (reductive and non-reductive) can be found in the essay "On the Nature of the Psychic" ( Jung, 1947/1954), where he takes into account both biological components (for example, instinctive "behavior patterns" in animals and humans) and their connection with non-causal principles associated with particle physics.

This question brings us directly to the philosophical position known as “ two-aspect monism ”, first adopted by Spinoza (see  Ravven, 2013 ), then, many years ago, by Jung and Pauli ( Atmanspacher, 2012 ), and recently re-proposed by Mark Solms et al. neuropsychoanalysts ( Kaplan-Solms and Solms, 2000 ). According to this point of view, the material and subjective worlds are two complementary manifestations of a unique, although perhaps unknowable unitary reality, which Jung describes with the concept of "psychoid". The presence of such a fundamental dimension is widely emphasized in the Eastern cultural tradition, as well as in some Western philosophers, such as A.N. Withehead (1929). In the 20th century, the existence of this dimension was discovered during the study of quantum mechanical processes in physics and unconscious processes in analytical psychology 10 . Both disciplines recognized the influence of invisible (paradoxical) phenomena within the normal flow of visible material and mental events.

Today, the application of nonlinear dynamic theories in neuroscience ( Freeman, 1999 ;  Brown, 2002 ;  Llinas, 2002 ;  Krieger, 2014 ), theoretically opens the way to the transition from the physics of massive bodies to quantum physics, and, therefore, to the level of reality in which synchronistic phenomena can be rationally acceptable – shifting the level of neurological analysis from the material-neurochemical level to the level of the immaterial-electric field,  11  ( Jung, 1955 ;  Bohm, 1980/1981 ;  Penrose, 1989 ;  Brown, 2002 ). For example, the neurologist  Brown (2002), who integrated Whitehead's processual philosophy with the latest discoveries of neuroscience, sees the Self as a microgenetic process of quantum oscillations rising from the depths (subcortex) of the brain to the surface (cortical level), giving rise to moments of consciousness that gradually develop from pure objectless excitation to complex representations and self-representations 12 .

According to such speculative hypotheses, we can represent  the nuclear affects  as "archetypes as such" acting as primary organized configurations of internal evaluative events that manifest both in brain-behavioral actions/dispositional patterns (objective domain) and in intensive affective feelings (subjective sphere). Such  archetypal psychotic  events are likely, in a broad sense at the physical descriptive level, consistent with the dynamical structure of quantum microwaves occurring in PKSS 13  (see  Brown, 2002 ). As amplification of quantum microwaves ( Penrose, 1989 ;  Brown, 2002) the brain can be more precisely considered as a transformer station that connects mind and matter and actualizes psychological processes in a linear space-time dimension. As Jung suggested:

"It can be assumed that the psyche gradually rises from a low extent to an infinite tension, going beyond the limits of, for example, the speed of light, and thereby derealizing the body. […] In the light of this approach, the brain can be a transformer station, where the relatively infinite tension or tension of the psyche is transformed into perceptible frequencies or "lengths". Conversely, the fading of introspective perception of the body is explained by gradual "psychization", that is, intensification due to expansion. Psyche = the highest intensity in the smallest space. Affects as primary organizing factors of subjective life

Recent neuroscience data confirm that affectivity is an embryonic form of subjectivity. More precisely, it was recognized that affects express an extended, diffused and primary process-related sensibility, devoid of any specific content or clear cognitive distinction between the external-objective and internal-subjective world. Such objectless and primary process-related sensibility is also called " anoetic"  consciousness (or proto-consciousness), "[…] a non-thinking form of experience that can be affectively intense without being known" ( Solms and Panksepp, 2012 , p. 149) 15. It is "a rudimentary state of autonomous consciousness […] with a fundamental form of first-person 'self-experience' based on affective experiential states and raw sensory and perceptual mental existence" ( Vandekerckhove and Panksepp, 2009 , p. 1). In this context, anoetic protoconscious feelings arise as a neurodynamic "top of a wave" from a continuous flow of diffuse excitation without a focus or intention (mood)  16 .

In fact, affectivity was considered a fundamental feature of the brain-mind by many early pioneers, including John Gulings Jackson, as well as Eugen Bleuler, one of Jung's most important partners in his scientific career. This is what Bleuler wrote in his landmark work on psychoses:

"[…] Affectivity is fully developed in a small child; all the affects that are present in an adult are fully developed in her. Conversely, the child's intelligence is devoid of content, and logical processes are relatively weak" ( Bleuler, 1906/1912 , p. 24, our translation).

Jung also, from the very beginning (that is, from his Studies of Associations  ( Jung, 1904/1905 )) considered affects and affective feelings to be absolutely relevant (relevant) to subjective mental life, which is emphasized in the following passage:

"Each mental process has its own tone of feelings (affective coloring) associated with it. This indicates the degree of influence of the process on the subject or how significant this process is for him (to the extent that this process generally reaches consciousness). It is through "affect" that the subject becomes involved and feels the full weight of reality" ( Jung, 1959/1978 , p. 61).

Thus, Jung viewed affects as forces that attract the subject and are experienced in the form of characteristic feelings. In this sense,  Jung (1928/1948 ) replaced Freud's theory of impulses (drives) with a much more convincing theory of the binding force of the field (in psychological terms - affect), which polarizes patterns or mental representations and endows them with value.

Therefore, affects can be considered as transpersonal forms of experience that penetrate the primary subjective field, which is not yet individual consciousness (or self-awareness), because it lives in an undifferentiated organism/environment continuum. At the same time, affects are a bridge between the collective and individual psyche, between the instinctive inheritance of the species and personal experience accumulated in individual life. As a member of this research team describes:

“By providing a common neural platform for a variety of affective experiences, the nuclear Self can be considered a nomothetic (universal) function of the brain. Since the nuclear Self, along with the many raw feelings it develops, interacts with higher cognitive [...] processes, it (the Self) contributes to the emergence of various ideographic (individually unique, improved by experience) expanded selves in the process of brain maturation" ( Panksepp and Biven, 2012 , page 392).

Although affects are initially objectless experiences, they provide a field through which all incoming perceptual stimuli/experiences are integrated into a single conscious and intentional state. Moreover, affective states powerfully modulate any form of individual learning and associative memory (a property of learning modulation) ( Panksepp and Biven, 2012 ;  Vandekerckhove et al., 2014 ). Thus, all personal experience accumulated during individual history takes the form of clusters (or complexes) of perceptual traces of memory (engrams) tending to affect. This idea was developed by  Jung (1960) in the form of the concept of "emotionally colored complexes", which he considered as psychological structures uniting different mental contents and representations on the basis of a common affective state 17 . Each complex is united by the same emotion that defines the core of its content and organizes experience, perception and affect around a constant central theme. For example, an inferiority complex is a constellation of memories, thoughts, and fantasies associated with a lack of self-worth, self-doubt, and self-doubt, as well as feelings of not meeting standards.

Thus, speaking of the complex, Jung wrote: "The constellating power of its nuclear element corresponds to  its value tension , that is, its energy" ( Jung, 1928/1948 , §19, Jung's italics). In the same spirit, he also wrote: "It would be an unforgivable sin and simplification if we did not notice the  emotional value of the archetype . This is extremely important both theoretically and therapeutically" [ZT 8, p. 411], since the archetype is "[…] a living system of reactions and tendencies" connected to a living personality "[…] a bridge of emotions" [ZT 18 , p. 589].

The Jungian theory of emotionally colored complexes is a development of Pierre Jeanet's work on autonomous fixed ideas. According to  Janet (1889) , fixed ideas are mental images or thoughts that have a high emotional charge and acquire exaggerated proportions, so they may not be integrated into the ego-consciousness in the usual way, and become isolated from the usual personality, creating dissociated states of mind ( Monahan, 2009 ). However, in contrast to Jeanet's original theory, Jung argued that the dissociative aspect of complexes is usually reversed, so they can be integrated to one degree or another according to the current situation. Only in severe mental pathologies, such as psychosis, certain complexes are permanently dissociated from the conscious "I", and the personality becomes fragmented.

The multi-layered neuro-evolutionary architecture of the Self

Many authors hypothesized that the Self is not a holistic phenomenon, and that it can be differentiated as layers that were formed in the process of evolution ( James, 1890/1950 ). Psychoanalysts have widely used the archaeological metaphor to describe the human mind. For example, Sigmund Freud saw the psyche as an ancient city, like Rome, where the passage of time was reflected in monumental stratifications from antiquity to the modern era. Studies of the multi-layered organization of the human psyche have been empirically enriched thanks to the contribution of neuroscience. John Huglings Jackson, the father of English neurology, was probably the first neurophysiologist to propose a clear hierarchical description of the functioning of the brain (see  Franz and Gillett, 2011 ), and his interesting conjectures were further developed in the works of  Luria (1962). Moreover, by integrating human and animal neuroanatomical perspectives,  MacLean (1990)  offered an important neuroevolutionary perspective on modern neuroscience (although not without extensive debate – see their review,  Panksepp, 2002 ). He conceptualized three overlapping layers in the human brain: the neocortical-rational brain characteristic of our species, the intermediate limbic-emotional brain characteristic of all mammals, and the visceral-instinctive brain characteristic of reptiles—a pattern that is more or less preserved among vertebrates 18 .

However, contrary to the dominant anthropocentric view, the accumulating evidence base of neuroscience confirms that a primary form of subjectivity is already present at the first level of the brain hierarchy (the instinctive brain of reptilian-ancient mammals) and that it is widespread among mammals, birds and possibly other vertebrates ( Panksepp, 1998b ,  2016 ;  Seth et al., 2005 ;  Northoff and Panksepp, 2008 ;  Edelman and Seth, 2009 ;  Panksepp and Northoff, 2009 ;  Revonsuo, 2010 ;  Ward, 2011 ;  Panksepp and Biven, 2011 2012 ;  Fabbro et al., 2015 ;  Feinberg and Mallatt, 2016). Growing evidence led a member of this research group to propose some radical changes to Paul McLean's tripartite neuroevolutionary model 19  ( Panksepp, 1998b ,  2011 ;  Panksepp and Biven, 2012 ) and prompted the modern neuropsychoanalytic movement to consider the reality of affective (if not cognitive-reflexive) "conscious ID" 20  ( Solms and Panksepp, 2012 ;  Solms, 2013 ).

According to such hypotheses, it was recently proposed that the phenomenon of consciousness can be differentiated as anoetic, noetic, and autonoetic states corresponding to different layers of the neuropsychic architecture ( Vandekerckhove and Panksepp, 2009 ,  2011 ).

Anoetic consciousness (already described in the previous paragraphs) is the first primary layer of the Self 21 , an ancient neuroevolutionary product widely distributed among animal species, ranging from mammals to birds and reptiles and possibly all vertebrates ( Fabbro et al., 2015 ;  Feinberg and Mallatt, 2016 ). Moreover, newborns are born with the inherited capacity to experience anoetic states as a result of the activity of subcortical regions of the brain that are already functionally mature at birth ( Merker, 2007 ), as we describe in this article.

On the other hand, noetic and autonoetic consciousness are newer evolutionary functions of the higher cortical and limbic areas of the brain and require a certain form of individual learning for adequate development and expression.

Noetic consciousness reflects experience, accumulated knowledge-based experience, which occurs when improved attentional abilities allow to clearly distinguish and classify specific features of the environment, which, with sufficient preservation of the neocortex, allows animals to think ahead. Indeed, when specific aspects of events are in focus, explicit object-reflexive awareness comes to the fore, while semantic (conceptual) memory helps analyze and categorize the situation ( Tulving, 1985 ). This form of consciousness, which Edelman called "primary consciousness", and which he associated with the activity of reverse thalamo-cortical brain circuits ( Edelman, 1989 ). Indirect evidence suggests that noetic consciousness is present in mammals, birds, and possibly also in reptiles (Edelman et al., 2005 ), and that human infants born very immature begin to show it after the third month of development.

The next step in neuro-psychic evolution is carried out by acquiring language ( Hauser et al., 2002 ;  Gazzaniga, 2011 ) and the ability to mentally travel through time ( Corballis, 2012 ), which makes possible not only the conscious recall of past events (episodic memory) , but also imagining future events, including one's own death ( Tulving, 2002 ). This narrative structure of the Self, which probably appeared more than 1 million years ago, most clearly (to us) manifested in the genus  Homo habilis , gives rise to the most complex autonoetic consciousness based on self-awareness, which Edelman described as "secondary consciousness" ( Edelman, 1992). Interestingly, autonoetic consciousness seems to be closely related to so-called "theory of mind" (the ability to imagine what is going on in other people's minds), which is apparently a fundamentally human trait ( Corballis, 2012 ).

The transition from the affective nuclear Self (anoetic consciousness) to the higher stages of the evolution of the Self (noetic and autonoetic consciousness) is an evolutionary leap that humans have made, a leap that was prepared long ago by earlier evolutionary processes in other mammals and perhaps birds and other creatures. In the following works, we will investigate and describe such evolutionary achievements, as well as the new creative potential that is opened by the acquisition of cognitive and reflective abilities 22 . Here we have focused on the most basic foundations of our existence – the affective substrates from which all our higher psychological functions originate. These initial layers of our minds provide an important foundation for all subsequent higher-order psychoneurological functions that make the human mind unique.

Conclusion

Although dominant neurocognitive paradigms tend to associate subjective life with the highest levels of brain organization (primarily the accumulation of individualized memories stored in the neuroplastic circuits of the forebrain), a large body of neuroethological evidence shows that non-human animals (mammals , birds and possibly other vertebrates) also have forms of subjectivity arising from the activity of the ancient evolutionary subcortical brainstem, diencephalon, and basal forebrain ( Panksepp, 1998b ,  2015 ,  2016 ;  Seth et al., 2005 ;  Edelman and Seth, 2009 ;  Revonsuo, 2010 ;  Ward, 2011 ; Panksepp and Biven, 2012 ; Fabbro et al., 2015 ). These findings clearly indicate that subjectivity is an inherited disposition directed at the instinctive, archaic bases of our brains ( Goodwyn, 2010 ), and they support Jung's view that  before  infants develop reflexive self-awareness , there is already a primal-instinctive affective form of the Self, which is expressed in the form of affective-psychic intentionality, which can efficiently and evaluatively interact with the material, deterministic world.

In his famous autobiography ( Jung, 1962/1963 ), Carl G. Jung describes his own dream, which he considered the most important revelation about the structure of the psyche:

"I was in a house I did not know, which had two floors. It was "my home" . I found myself on the upper floor, which was like a living room, furnished with exquisite antiques in the Rococo style. Many precious old paintings hung on the walls. I marveled that this was my house and thought, "Not bad." But then it occurred to me that I don't know what the bottom floor looks like.

Descending the stairs, I reached the first floor. Everything was much older there. I realized that this part of the house must date from about the 15th or 16th century. The furniture was medieval, the floors were made of red brick. It was quite dark everywhere. I went from room to room thinking, "Now I really have to explore the whole house." I came upon a heavy door and opened it. Behind them I discovered stone steps that led to the cellar.

Descending again, I found myself in a beautiful vaulted room, which looked exceedingly ancient. Examining the walls, I saw layers of bricks between ordinary stone blocks and fragments of bricks in lime mortar. As soon as I saw it, I knew that these walls date back to Roman times. Now my interest became very strong. I looked more closely at the floor. They were stone blocks, and in one of them I discovered a ring. When I pulled it, the stone slab lifted and I saw again the narrow stone steps leading down into the depths.

I also descended through them and entered a low cave cut in the rock. There was thick dust on the floor, and bones and broken pottery were scattered in the dust, like remnants of a primitive culture. I discovered two human skulls - obviously very old, they were half-disintegrated. Then I woke up" ( Jung, 1962/1963 , p. 155).

Such a dream is another example of how the psyche can be composed of many neural layers, with the superficial and recent layers being formed on the basis of older layers - formed during the development of the natural (and cultural) history of our species. Reflecting on the meaning of the deepest and most primitive layer, the cave, Jung wrote:

“[Here] we reach the bare bedrock, and with it the prehistoric times when reindeer hunters struggled for their bare and miserable existence against the elements of wild nature. People of that era still fully possessed animal instincts, without which life would be impossible" (63, p. 55).
