Code of ethics

This code governs all Individual Members (who do not belong to IAAP member communities) and Routers (anyone who is training towards individual membership) of the International Association for Analytical Psychology (IAAP). All IAAP Individual Members and Routers are required to read and accept the Code of Ethics. Analytical psychologists in their work must meet high ethical standards and observe the interests of their patients first and foremost. This is the most important part of the analytical psychologist's responsibility.

Violation of any of these IAAP ethical rules constitutes serious misconduct. Failure to comply with the Code of Ethics for Individual Members may result in suspension or termination of individual or group membership in the IAAP.

I. The relationship between the analyst and the client

Responsibility towards clients:

The analyst respects the integrity and personal boundaries of his client under any circumstances, except in cases of clear and significant threat to the patient, requiring urgent intervention without his consent.

A) At the beginning of the work, the analyst clearly informs the client about the terms and rules of therapy, for example, about the time, frequency of sessions and terms of payment. The analyst must ensure that these terms and conditions are accepted.

B) Financial calculations should be limited to professional payment (fee).

C) Social contacts with the client during therapy should be limited. After the end of the therapy, one should also take into account the tendency to store transference feelings for a long time and keep a reasonable distance in social contacts. Social contacts with relatives should be established with great care and only with the knowledge and consent of the client. In certain cases, exceptions are possible, for example, if the client poses a danger to himself and / or others, when working with children, or if the agreed therapy plan includes meetings with family and loved ones for therapeutic or counseling purposes.

D) Confidentiality and preservation of the client's anonymity are extremely important. Special care should be taken when publishing and presenting clinical material at seminars. If a client requests that their material not be published or submitted, their opinion must be respected. Care should also be taken when using professional advice.

An exception may be made in cases where a criminal case is initiated or when the law provides for a violation of confidentiality, as in the case of child abuse, the need to warn of harm to others, by court order, etc.

D) An analytical psychologist should not enter into sexual relations with his clients and not seek any personal benefits that violate the analytical framework (or boundaries). Interrupting a therapeutic relationship in order to engage in sexual intercourse is also unethical.

E) Analytical psychologist should not use physical coercion in relation to the client. Physical coercion is possible in cases where the client represents a physical threat and may be limited in actions.

Area of ​​personal responsibility:

F) An analytical psychologist should not claim a qualification that he does not possess.

Q) An analytical psychologist must respect the copyright of colleagues, make appropriate references and refrain from plagiarism.

I) An analytical psychologist must not continue to practice under the serious or permanent influence of (1) alcohol or other substances or (2) physical or psychological illness or severe stress that may affect the adequacy of his judgment and actions and impair his ability to perform professional work.

J) If an analytical psychologist is accused of committing a criminal offense or of unethical behavior by the professional community or licensing authorities of the state or country in which he resides, he must inform the President of the IAAP of these events, providing the relevant facts.

II. The relationship between an analyst and a supervisor or a training analyst and an analyst in training (Control Analyst / Control Analysand)

Responsibilities of supervisors or analysts in training:

The supervisor or training analyst must respect the special relationship established with supervisees or analysts in training. A supervisor or training analyst may not use his or her authority to establish sexual relations, exploit or otherwise gain advantage in relation to the person he or she is supposed to assess, examine, qualify or present for qualification. The same restrictions should be followed even after the end of the training/supervision relationship, given the tendency for long-term dynamics of transference and projection relationships that arise in the training process.

III. Duties in relation to the Ethics Committee

The duty of the Individual Member to seek help and inform about unprofessional behavior:

A) An individual IAAP Member is required to seek assistance from and report to the IAAP Ethics Committee on instances of unprofessional conduct. The report itself does not exempt the member from responsibility for unethical behavior and does not relieve the member from disciplinary action by the Ethics Committee.

Duty to report unprofessional behavior of colleagues:

B) In the event that an IAAP analyst witnesses unethical behavior by a colleague, it is his duty to inform the IAAP Ethics Committee, except when patient confidentiality is involved. If an IAAP member witnesses unethical behavior by a colleague or router, he/she should first talk to that colleague/router and try to prevent the violation and, if necessary, offer counseling or referral for personal analysis. If the involved member cannot do so directly or is bound by confidentiality, he/she may refer to the Chair of the Ethics Committee.

Obligation of direct cooperation with the IAAP Ethics Committee:

C) In the event that an Individual Member or router is called upon to investigate a complaint or conflict in the investigation of an ethical violation, refusal to meet with and cooperate with the IAAP Ethics Committee shall not in itself constitute grounds for a charge of unethical or unprofessional conduct. Such refusal may constitute grounds for decision by the Committee, up to and including recommending suspension or termination of individual or group membership in the IAAP.

The first version of the IAAP Code of Ethics, with some additions and changes, was created on the basis of ethical codes, guidelines and procedures adopted by the Society of Analytical Psychology, London (Society of Analytical Psychology - London, February 1991), the Jung Institute in San Francisco (the CG Jung Institute of San Francisco, April 1991), and the New York Association for Analytical Psychology (New York Association for Analytical Psychology, November 1983 and November 1986 editions) .

Eli B. Weisstub, MD, FRCPC

July 1992
