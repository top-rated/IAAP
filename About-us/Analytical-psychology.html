Analytical psychology

What is analytical psychology?

Analytical psychology is an approach to psychotherapy and in-depth analysis from the standpoint of the tradition founded by the Swiss psychiatrist K.G. Jung As Jung defined it from the beginning, it is distinguished by its emphasis on the role of symbolic and spiritual experience in human life and is based on Jungian theories of archetypes and the existence of a deep psychic space or collective unconscious. Research based on Jung's original work, within his tradition, draws on the advances of other disciplines and schools of depth psychology, making analytical psychology a dynamic and evolving field of research and therapeutic innovation.

The goal of Jungian analysis is what Jung called individuation. Individuation should not be confused simply with individuality or eccentricity. Rather, individuation means achieving a better understanding of the factors that influence how a person interacts with the totality of his psychological, interpersonal, and cultural experiences. Jung defined two deep levels of psychological functioning, which mainly shape, color and sometimes negatively affect the life experience of a person. Like Freud, Jung recognized the importance of early life experiences, which he called personal complexes arising from disruptions in the course of a person's life and all contained in the personal unconscious. However, Jung's particular insight was his recognition that we are also influenced by factors outside of our personal experience that are more universal in nature.

The analytical process is aimed at bringing these factors (both personal and collective) to the level of consciousness, which will allow a person to see more clearly what forces play their role in his life. Jung's understanding of archetypes in particular involves a sense of "telos" or purpose toward which life can be directed. The analyst's role is to facilitate the individuation process and accompany the analysand on his personal journey.
